Section 1.6 Basis and dimension

¶

Subsection 1.6.1 Span and independence

In the last section we saw that solutions of \(\bA\bx=\bzero\) play an important role in all linear systems.

Definition 1.6.1. Nullspace.

The nullspace of a matrix \(\bA\in\Rmn{m}{n}\) is

\begin{equation} \null(\bA) = \{ \bx\in\Rn{n} : \bA\bx=\bzero \}\text{.}\label{la-eq-nullspace}\tag{1.6.1} \end{equation}

We also saw that every element of \(\null(\bA)\) can be expressed as a linear combination, \(c_1\bx_1+\cdots+c_k\bx_k\text{.}\)

Definition 1.6.2. Span.

The span of vectors \(\bx_1,\ldots,\bx_k\) is

\begin{equation} \span(\bx_1,\ldots,\bx_k) = \{ c_1\bx_1+\cdots+c_k\bx_k : c_1,\ldots,c_k \in \mathbb{R} \}\text{.}\label{la-eq-span}\tag{1.6.2} \end{equation}

The notion of span gives us a shorthand for expressing the nullspace (equivalently, the general homogeneous solution). For reasons we won't get into, every span is referred to as a space. However, it's important to know that there are infinitely many different ways to express the same space. For instance,

\begin{equation*} \span\left(\bx_1\right) = \span\left(\bx_1,\bx_1,\bx_1\right)\text{.} \end{equation*}

That's kind of an obvious “cheat”. Here is a more subtle one:

\begin{equation*} \span\left( \twovec{1}{0}, \twovec{0}{1}, \twovec{1}{1} \right) = \span\left( \twovec{1}{0}, \twovec{0}{1} \right)\text{.} \end{equation*}

The reasoning here is that the first two vectors already span all of \(\Rn{2}\text{,}\) so there's nothing more that the third vector can bring to the table. We have a term for these observations.

Definition 1.6.3. Linear independence.

The vectors \(\bx_1,\dots,\bx_k\) are linearly dependent if there are scalars \(c_1,\ldots,c_k\text{,}\) not all zero, such that

\begin{equation*} c_1\bx_1+\cdots+c_k\bx_k = \bzero\text{.} \end{equation*}

Otherwise the vectors are linearly independent.

Why is this the right concept? Well, if the vectors are dependent, then by definition we can write

\begin{equation*} c_j \bx_j = - \sum_{i\neq j} c_i \bx_i\text{,} \end{equation*}

where \(c_j\neq 0\text{.}\) If we divide this through by \(c_j\text{,}\) then we have shown that \(\bx_j\) lies within the span of all the other vectors, and therefore including it with linear combinations is redundant.

Subsection 1.6.2 Independence and homogeneous systems

¶

Matters of linearly independence can be restated in terms of homogeneous linear systems (and vice versa). That is because of the equivalence between linear combinations of vectors and matrix-vector multiplication:

\begin{equation*} \sum_{j=1}^k c_j \bx_j = c_1\bx_1+\cdots+c_k\bx_k = \begin{bmatrix} \bx_1 \amp \cdots \amp \bx_k \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} c_1 \\ \vdots \\ c_k \end{bmatrix} = \bX \bc. \end{equation*}

Hence the question “Are \(\bx_1,\dots,\bx_k\) independent?” is equivalent to “Does \(\bX\bc=\bzero\) have any nonzero solutions?” Because homogeneous problems always have at least one solution (the zero vector), this question is also equivalent to “Does \(\bX\bc=\bzero\) have infinitely many solutions?”

Example 1.6.4.

Suppose we want to check the independence of

\begin{equation*} \bx_1 = \threevec{1}{2}{-1}, \quad \bx_2 = \threevec{2}{4}{-2}, \quad \bx_3 = \threevec{-1}{1}{4}\text{.} \end{equation*}

We assemble these as the columns of a matrix

\begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 2 \amp -1 \\ 2 \amp 4 \amp 1 \\ -1 \amp -2 \amp 4 \end{bmatrix} \text{.} \end{equation*}

(The order of columns doesn't matter to independence.) Technically we ought to tack on a zero column to get the augmented matrix, but row operations can never change that column. So we can find the RREF without it:

\begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 2 \amp -1 \\ 2 \amp 4 \amp 1 \\ -1 \amp -2 \amp 4 \end{bmatrix} \mapsto \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 1 \amp -1 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 3 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 3 \end{bmatrix} \mapsto \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 1 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 1 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}

(We could have actually stopped before the end, when it became clear that the second column is not a pivot column.) Thus the homogeneous problem will have at least one free parameter.

Subsection 1.6.3 Basis

¶

Put span and independence together and you get something good.

Definition 1.6.5. Basis.

If \(\bx_1,\dots,\bx_k\) are independent, then they form a basis of \(\span(\bx_1,\dots,\bx_k)\text{.}\)

It turns out that every basis of a given space has the same number of vectors in it. This number is called the dimension of the space.

Example 1.6.6.

We find the RREF of a \(3\times 3\) matrix:

\begin{equation*} \bA = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 2 \amp -1 \\ 2 \amp 4 \amp 1 \\ -1 \amp -2 \amp 4 \end{bmatrix} \mapsto \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 1 \amp -1 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 3 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 3 \end{bmatrix} \mapsto \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 1 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 1 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 0 \end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Columns 1 and 3 are the pivot columns. We regard \(x_2\) as a free variable; say \(x_2=s\text{.}\) Then \(x_3=0\) and \(x_1=-s\text{,}\) and the homogeneous general solution is

\begin{equation*} \bx_h = \begin{bmatrix} -s \\ s \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} = s \begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Equivalently, we say that

\begin{equation*} \null(\bA) = \span\left(\begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}\right)\text{,} \end{equation*}

and in fact \([-1,1,0]\) is a basis of the nullspace, which is therefore one-dimensional.

MATLAB has a null command that produces a basis of the nullspace.

This is equivalent to the answer we gave above, just with a different normalization of the vector.

Example 1.6.8.

Suppose the RREF of a matrix ends with

\begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 0 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 1 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}

(i.e., an identity matrix). There are no free variables, and the only solution is \(\bx=[0,0,0]\text{.}\) Hence \(\null(\bA) = \{\bzero\}\text{.}\) This singleton set does not have a basis and is considered to have dimension zero. (Try this example in MATLAB!)

I emphasize that we have done no new math here. It's still just about linear systems and RREF matrices, with different and more precise terminology.
