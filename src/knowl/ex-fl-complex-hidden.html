Suppose we are given the purely real problem \(y'+y = 2 \sin(t) - 2 \cos(t)\text{.}\) Formula (3) on page 31 gives a full solution to this problem. But it's useful to see how complex formulation can help us do without formulas.

First, look at the forcing function. As a combination of sine and cosine at frequency 1, we can write it as a lagged cosine, \(R\cos(t-\phi)\text{.}\) The values \((R,\phi)\) are the polar form of the complex number \(-2+2i\text{.}\) It takes only a moment to deduce that \(R=\sqrt{8}\) and \(\phi=3\pi/4\text{.}\)

Next, the shifted cosine is the real part of the complex forcing function \(R\exp[i(t-\phi)]\text{.}\) To get a particular solution, then, we multiply the forcing by \(1/(i\omega-a)= 1/(i+1)\text{.}\) We can use "realization of the denominator" to find that

\begin{equation*} \frac{1}{i + 1} = \frac{1}{i + 1} \frac{-i+1}{-i + 1} = \frac{1-i}{2}\text{.} \end{equation*}

This is also easy to put into polar form. So the complex particular solution is

\begin{align*} y = \frac{1}{i+1} R e^{i(t-\phi)} \amp = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} e^{-i\pi/4} \sqrt{8} e^{i(t-3\pi/4)}\\ \amp = 2 e^{i(t-\pi)} = 2 e^{it} e^{-i\pi} = -2e^{it}\text{.} \end{align*}

Finally, the real particular solution is the real part of this, which is simply \(-2\cos(t)\text{.}\)
