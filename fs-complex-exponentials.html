Section 3.4 Complex exponentials

¶

How are we going to make sense of \(e^{\lambda t}\bv\) when \(\lambda\) is not a real number? This question leads to some really profound and useful mathematics.

Let's start with the simplest, scalar case, \(e^{it}\text{.}\) This should be the solution of \(x'=ix\text{,}\) \(x(0)=1\text{.}\) If we assume that \(x(t)\) is complex-valued, let us write it as

\begin{equation*} x(t) = u(t) + i v(t)\text{,} \end{equation*}

for real \(u,v\text{.}\) Then \(x'=ix\) becomes

\begin{equation*} u' + iv' = i(u+iv) = i^2v + iu = -v + iu\text{.} \end{equation*}

This leads to the coupled real equations

\begin{equation*} u' = -v, \quad v'=u\text{,} \end{equation*}

together with \(u(0)=1\text{,}\) \(v(0)=0\text{.}\) It won't take you long to verify that \(u(t)=\cos(t)\text{,}\) \(v(t)=\sin(t)\) is the solution. So this implies the identity

Fact 3.4.1. Euler's identity.
\begin{equation} e^{it} = \cos(t) + i\sin(t)\text{.}\label{fs-eq-euler}\tag{3.4.1} \end{equation}

This is one of the big and fundamental surprises of applied math: exponential functions, when you go in an imaginary direction, become sin/cos oscillations. We can illustrate this with MATLAB, which handles complex values routinely. Note the use of 1i to get the imaginary unit.

Subsection 3.4.1 Polar form of complex numbers

¶

Euler's identity (3.4.1) gives us an alternative to the rectangular decomposition \(z=u+iv\text{.}\) If \(r\) is a positive number, and we write

\begin{equation*} re^{i\theta} = (r\cos \theta)+i(r\sin \theta)\text{,} \end{equation*}

then it is clear that \((r,\theta)\) represent the polar coordinates of the point. Furthermore, the polar \(r\) is just the modulus \(|z|\text{.}\) (The angle \(\theta\) might variously be called the argument or phase of \(z\text{.}\))

In rectangular form, addition and subtraction of complex numbers is easy and naturally interpreted as vector addition in the plane. In polar form, it's multiplication and division that become easy:

\begin{equation*} (re^{i\theta})(se^{i\phi}) = rse^{i(\theta+\phi)}, \qquad \frac{re^{i\theta}}{se^{i\phi}} = \frac{r}{s}e^{i(\theta-\phi)}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Geometrically, multiplication by a complex number scales the magnitude and then rotates around the origin.

Subsection 3.4.2 Complex exponentials

¶

There is a key connection between the rectangular form of a complex number and the polar form of its exponential:

\begin{equation} e^{(a+i \omega)t} = e^{at} e^{i \omega t} = e^{at} (\cos \omega t + i \sin \omega t)\text{.}\label{fs-eq-complex-exp}\tag{3.4.2} \end{equation}

The first term in the product is the familiar real \(e^{at}\text{,}\) which is an amplitude that exponentially grows or decays in time (or stays fixed if \(a=0\)). The other term has modulus one and can be interpreted as a parameterization of the unit circle in the complex plane with constant angular velocity \(\omega\text{.}\)

Example 3.4.3.

Here are some looks at the function \(e^{\lambda t}\) for different complex values of \(\lambda\text{.}\) First, when \(\lambda\) is purely imaginary, the function values stay on the unit circle in the complex plane. Taking the real and imaginary parts of the function give cosine and sine, respectively.

If \(\Re(\lambda) \gt 0\text{,}\) the magnitude of the function grows exponentially. The result is an outward spiral. The imaginary part of \(\lambda\) controls the frequency or "tightness" of the spiral.

Finally, if \(\Re(\lambda) \lt 0\text{,}\) the spiral is a decaying one. The real and imaginary parts are attenuated oscillations.

Fact 3.4.7.

In the function \(e^{\lambda t}\text{,}\) the real part of \(\lambda\) determines exponential growth or decay of amplitude, and the imaginary part of \(\lambda\) determines constant frequency of oscillation.

Both purely real exponentials and purely harmonic sines and cosines are just special cases of the complex exponential!

Subsection 3.4.3 Converting to real solutions

¶

An applied mathematician is perfectly happy with a solution using complex numbers, even if it represents something that is actually real. But an engineer might get fired for not explaining things in real form to the sales team.

Let's consider the \(2\times 2\) case for simplicity. The general solution is

\begin{equation*} \bx(t) = \bX(t)\bc = c_1 e^{\lambda_1 t} \bv_1 + c_2 e^{\lambda_2 t} \bv_2\text{.} \end{equation*}

If \(\bA\) is real, then \(\lambda_2=\overline{\lambda_1}\) and \(\bv_2=\overline{\bv_1}\text{.}\) If the initial conditions are also real, then it works out that \(c_2=\overline{c_1}\) as well, and the entire solution can be expressed by expanding each item into real and imaginary parts. This is, as we say in the biz, not fun.

The pain is lessened if we use a different form of the general solution by writing out the real and imaginary parts of \(e^{\lambda_1 t} \bv_1\text{.}\) If \(\lambda_1=a+i\omega\text{,}\) then write

\begin{equation*} e^{\lambda_1 t} \bv_1 = e^{at} \bigl[\cos(\omega t)+i\sin(\omega t) \bigr] \bv_1 = e^{at} \bigl[ u(t) + i w(t) \bigr]\text{.} \end{equation*}

Then an alternative expression for the general solution is \(\bx(t)=b_1 e^{at} u(t) + b_2 e^{at} w(t)\text{,}\) where everything in that formula is now real-valued.

Example 3.4.8.

We eariler found the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of

\begin{equation*} \bA = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp -1 \\ 5 \amp -3 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}

to be

\begin{equation*} \lambda_{1,2} = -1 \pm 1i, \qquad \bv_{1,2} = \twovec{1}{2\mp i}\text{.} \end{equation*}

This gives a general solution in the complex form

\begin{equation*} \bx(t) = c_1 e^{(-1+i)t} \twovec{1}{2- i} + c_2 e^{(-1-i)t} \twovec{1}{2+i}\text{.} \end{equation*}

It's implied that \(c_{1,2}\) are complex constants.

To find an alternative real form, we write

\begin{align*} e^{(-1+i)t} \twovec{1}{2-i} \amp = e^{-t} [\cos(t)+i\sin(t)] \left( \twovec{1}{2} + i\twovec{0}{-1} \right)\\ \amp = e^{-t} \left( \cos(t)\twovec{1}{2} - \sin(t) \twovec{0}{-1} \right)\\ \amp \qquad + i \left( \cos(t)\twovec{0}{-1} + \sin(t) \twovec{1}{2} \right) \text{.} \end{align*}

Thus the general solution can also be written as

\begin{equation*} \bx(t) = b_1 e^{-t} \twovec{\cos(t)}{2\cos(t)+\sin(t)} + b_2 e^{-t}\twovec{\sin(t)}{2\sin(t)-\cos(t)} \text{,} \end{equation*}

where here \(b_1\) and \(b_2\) are real.
