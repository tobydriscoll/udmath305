Section 1.11 Eigenvalues

¶

The last stop on our whirlwind tour of linear algebra is a topic that is very important to the differential equations we study in future chapters. (I say “very important” here in the sense of oxygen being a very important part of breathing.)

Everything in this section still relates only to square matrices.

Definition 1.11.1. Eigenvalue and eigenvector.

Suppose \(\bA\in\Cmn{n}{n}\text{.}\) If there exist a scalar \(\lambda\) and a nonzero vector \(\bv\) such that

\begin{equation*} \bA \bv = \lambda \bv\text{,} \end{equation*}

then \(\lambda\) is an eigenvalue of \(\bA\) with associated eigenvector \(\bv\text{.}\)

If you think of \(\bA\) as acting on vectors, then an eigenvector is a direction in which the action of \(\bA\) is the same as a scalar; we have found a little one-dimensional oasis in which the behavior of \(\bA\) is easy to comprehend.

Subsection 1.11.1 Eigenspaces

¶

An eigenvalue is a clean, well-defined target. Eigenvectors can be slipperier. For starters, if \(\bA\bv=\lambda\bv\text{,}\) then

\begin{equation*} \bA(c\bv) = c(\bA\bv)=c(\lambda\bv)=\lambda(c\bv)\text{.} \end{equation*}

Hence every nonzero multiple of an eigenvector is also an eigenvector for the same \(\lambda\). But there can be even more ambiguity than that.

Example 1.11.2.

Let \(\bI\) be an identity matrix. Then \(\bI\bx=\bx\) for any vector \(\bx\text{,}\) so every nonzero vector is an eigenvector!

Fortunately we already have the tools we need to describe a more robust target, based on the very simple reformulation \(\bzero=\bA\bv-\lambda\bv=(\bA-\lambda\bI)\bv\text{.}\)

Definition 1.11.3. Eigenspace.

Let \(\lambda\) be an eigenvalue of \(\bA\text{.}\) The eigenspace associated with \(\lambda\) is \(\nullsp(\bA-\lambda\bI)\text{.}\)

Eigenspaces, unlike eigenvectors, are unique. We have to be a bit careful, though, because we usually express such spaces as the span of basis vectors, and those bases are not themselves unique. For instance, \(\spanset\left(\twovec{1}{0},\twovec{0}{1}\right)\) and \(\spanset\left(\twovec{1}{1},\twovec{-1}{1}\right)\) are both equal to \(\Cn{2}\text{.}\)

Note that if \(\lambda\) is not an eigenvalue, then (by definition) the only solution of \((\bA-\lambda\bI)\bv=\bzero\) is \(\bv=\bzero\text{.}\) In other words, the dimension of \(\nullsp(\bA-\lambda\bI)\) is zero, in which case (by Theorem 1.9.1) \(\bA-\lambda\bI\) is invertible.

Fact 1.11.4.

\(\lambda\) is an eigenvalue of \(\bA\) if and only if \(\bA-\lambda\bI\) is singular.

In practice the most common way to find eigenvalues by hand is through the equivalent condition \(\det(\bA-\lambda\bI)=0\text{.}\)

Example 1.11.5.

We look for eigenvalues of

\begin{equation*} \bA = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 1 \\ 4 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Start by computing

\begin{equation*} \left| \twomat{1}{1}{4}{1} - \twomat{\lambda}{0}{0}{\lambda} \right| = \twodet{1-\lambda}{1}{4}{1-\lambda} = (1-\lambda)^2 - 4 = \lambda^2-2\lambda-3\text{.} \end{equation*}

We find eigenvalues by setting this equal to zero, which means \(\lambda_1=3\) or \(\lambda_2=-1\text{.}\) These are the eigenvalues.

What about the eigenspaces? First consider \(\lambda_1=3\text{:}\)

\begin{equation*} \bA-3 \bI = \twomat{-2}{1}{4}{-2} \overset{\text{RREF}}{\Longrightarrow} \twomat{1}{-1/2}{0}{0}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Therefore this nullspace (the eigenspace) is \(\spanset\left(\twovec{1/2}{1}\right)\text{.}\) As for \(\lambda_2=-1\text{,}\)

\begin{equation*} \bA+ \bI = \twomat{2}{1}{4}{2} \overset{\text{RREF}}{\Longrightarrow} \twomat{1}{1/2}{0}{0}\text{,} \end{equation*}

leading to the eigenspace \(\spanset\left(\twovec{-1/2}{1}\right)\text{.}\)

MATLAB computes eigenvalues and eigenvectors (through an entirely different process) with the eig command.

In the second format (two outputs), the eigenvalues are on the diagonal of D. The corresponding columns of V are basis vectors for their eigenspaces, provided those are one-dimensional.

By hand you usually find eigenvalues by finding the roots of a low-degree polynomial. It turns out that when the size is greater than \(2\times 2\text{,}\) other linear algebra methods for eigenvalues are much easier than finding the polynomial roots. In fact, MATLAB uses eigenvalues to find polynomial roots, rather than the other way around!

In the \(2\times 2\) case, suppose \(\lambda\) is already known to be an eigenvalue. Let the first row of \(\bA-\lambda\bI\) be \([\alpha,\beta]\text{.}\) If \(\alpha=\beta=0\text{,}\) then the eigenspace is all of \(\Cn{2}\text{.}\) Otherwise, the eigenspace is \(\spanset\left(\twovec{-\beta}{\alpha}\right)\text{.}\)

In the general \(n\times n\) case, \(\det(\bA-\lambda\bI)\) is a polynomial of degree \(n\) whose roots are the eigenvalues of \(\bA\text{.}\) (This can be seen through the cofactor expansion definition of the determinant.)

Definition 1.11.7. Characteristic polynomial.

The characteristic polynomial of \(\bA\) is \(\det(\bA-\lambda\bI)\text{.}\)

Given what we know about roots of polynomials, there are some conclusions it's worth stating clearly.

Theorem 1.11.8. Eigenvalue properties.

Suppose \(\bA\) is an \(n\times n\) matrix. Then

  1. \(\bA\) has at most \(n\) distinct complex eigenvalues, and
  2. if \(\bA\) is real, then any complex eigenvalues occur in conjugate pairs, as do their associated eigenvectors.
Example 1.11.9.

Consider \(\bA=\twomat{4}{0}{1}{4}\text{.}\) The characteristic polynomial is

\begin{equation*} \twodet{4-\lambda}{0}{1}{4-\lambda} = (4-\lambda)^2\text{,} \end{equation*}

so \(\lambda_1=4\) is the only eigenvalue. Since

\begin{equation*} \bA - 4\bI = \twomat{0}{0}{1}{0}\text{,} \end{equation*}

the nullspace consists of all vectors \(\twovec{0}{s}\text{,}\) i.e., it is \(\spanset\left(\twovec{0}{1}\right)\text{.}\)

Example 1.11.10.

We find the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of

\begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp -1 \\ 5 \amp -3 \end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}

The characteristic polynomial is \(\lambda^2 +2 \lambda +2\text{,}\) with roots \(\lambda_{1,2} = -1 \pm 1i.\)

To find an eigenvector for \(\lambda_1\text{,}\) we note that the first row of \(\bA - \lambda_1 \mathbf{I} \) is \([(2-i),-1]\text{.}\) Thus a basis for this eigenspace is the single eigenvector \(\twovec{1}{2-i}\text{.}\)

We get a benefit here from the complex eigenvalues: the conjugate of an eigenvector for \(\lambda_1\) will be an eigenvector for \(\lambda_2\text{.}\) So we have \(\spanset\left(\twovec{1}{2+i}\right)\) to go with \(\lambda_2\text{.}\)

Subsection 1.11.2 Multiplicity

¶

You should be familiar with the idea of multiplicity for roots of a polynomial. We can factor a characteristic polynomial \(p\) to get

\begin{equation*} p(z) = (z-\lambda_1)^{m_1}(z-\lambda_2)^{m_2}\dots(z-\lambda_k)^{m_k} \end{equation*}

for nonnegative integer exponents such that \(m_1+\cdots+m_k=n\text{.}\) These exponents are the multiplicities of the roots.

Definition 1.11.11. Algebraic multiplicity.

The algebraic multiplicity of an eigenvalue is its multiplicity as a root of the characteristic polynomial.

But eigenvalues have another significant notion of multiplicity as well.

Definition 1.11.12. Geometric multiplicity.

The geometric multiplicity of an eigenvalue is the dimension of its associated eigenspace.

Fact 1.11.13.

The geometric multiplicity of an eigenvalue is less than or equal to its algebraic multiplicity.

Definition 1.11.14. Defective eigenvalue.

An eigenvalue \(\lambda\) whose geometric multiplicity is strictly less than its algebraic multiplicity is said to be defective.

Example 1.11.15.

The \(n\times n\) identity matrix has characteristic polynomial \(p(z)=(z-1)^n\) (by recursive cofactor expansion). Thus its only eigenvalue is \(\lambda=1\text{,}\) with algebraic multiplicity \(n\text{.}\) All nonzero vectors are eigenvectors, so the dimension of the eigenspace is also \(n\text{.}\) Hence \(\lambda=1\) is nondefective.

Example 1.11.16.

Example 1.11.9 showed that the matrix \(\bA=\twomat{4}{0}{1}{4}\) has eigenvalue \(\lambda_1=4\) with algebraic multiplicity 2 and geometric multiplicity 1. Thus this eigenvalue (and by extension the matrix itself) is defective.

Since multiplicities are always at least one, there is one simple but common case in which we are certain that there are no defective eigenvalues.

Theorem 1.11.17. Distinct eigenvalues imply nondefectiveness.

If \(\bA\in\Cmn{n}{n}\) has \(n\) distinct eigenvalues, then \(\bA\) has no defective eigenvalues.

This is not also an “only if” statement, though, as demonstrated by the identity matrix.
