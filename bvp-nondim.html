Section 6.5 Nondimensionalization

¶

Engineering problems come with lots of physical units: mass, length, reaction rate, diffusivity, etc. Some of these may be known imprecisely, or are available to tune the behavior of the system. It's important to get them all right, of course. But sometimes they can clutter the scene and obscure fundamental results. Also, it's difficult to explore high-dimensional parameter spaces efficiently when optimizing for some goal, so reducing the number of independent parameters can be useful.

The technique of nondimensionalization replaces the original variables, which have SI or (shudder) American units associated with them, by variables that are "pure numbers" with no units attached. This is done by scaling them by reference amounts, typically intrinsic scales, that make the parameters of the resulting problem as simple as we can make them.

Subsection 6.5.1 First-order ODEs

Our first example is the linear ODE \(ay'+by=f(t)\text{.}\) We define new variables via

\begin{equation*} t = \tau T, \quad y = \eta Y, \end{equation*}

for dimensional constants \(\tau\) and \(\eta\) to be determined later. The chain rule implies

\begin{equation*} \dd{}{t} = \frac{1}{\tau} \dd{}{T}. \end{equation*}

Hence

\begin{equation*} \frac{a\eta}{\tau} \dd{Y}{T} + b \eta Y = f \text{,} \end{equation*}

or

\begin{equation*} \dd{Y}{T} + \frac{b\tau}{a} Y = \frac{\tau}{a\eta} f\text{.} \end{equation*}

The idea is to make our lives as easy as possible. So we define

\begin{equation*} \tau = \frac{a}{b}, \quad \eta = \frac{\tau}{a} = \frac{1}{b}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Then

\begin{equation*} \dd{Y}{T} + Y = F(T) \text{,} \end{equation*}

where \(F(T)=f(t)=f(T/b)\text{.}\) We can reason all we want about this simpler equation and always translate the results back to physical units by applying the unit definitions we made. Note that the time scale we chose is the inverse of the exponential decay rate of the original problem; in fact, it is proportional to the half-life of the system.

Subsection 6.5.2 Second-order ODE

Next consider a driven oscillator,

\begin{equation*} m \ddd{y}{t} + b \dd{y}{t} + k y = A \cos(\omega t)\text{.} \end{equation*}

We again use new variables defined by

\begin{equation*} t = \tau T, \quad y = \eta Y, \end{equation*}

with dimensionless \(T\) and \(Y\text{.}\) Then

\begin{equation*} \frac{m\eta}{\tau^2} \ddd{Y}{T} + \frac{b\eta}{\tau} \dd{Y}{T} + k \eta Y = A \cos(\omega \tau T)\text{,} \end{equation*}

thus

\begin{equation*} \ddd{Y}{T} + \frac{b\tau}{m} \dd{Y}{T} + \frac{k\tau^2}{m} Y = \frac{A\tau^2}{m\eta} \cos(\omega \tau T)\text{.} \end{equation*}

With only \(\tau\) and \(\eta\) at our disposal, we can't simplify both the first-order and zeroth-order coefficients. We choose

\begin{equation*} \frac{k\tau^2}{m} = 1 \quad \Rightarrow \quad \tau = \sqrt{\frac{m}{k}} = \frac{1}{\omega_0}\text{,} \end{equation*}

using our old definition of natural frequency. We also use

\begin{equation*} \frac{A\tau^2}{m\eta} = 1 \quad \Rightarrow \quad \eta = \frac{A\tau^2}{m} = \frac{A}{k}\text{.} \end{equation*}

So now

\begin{equation*} \ddd{Y}{T} + \frac{b}{k\tau} \dd{Y}{T} + Y = \cos(\omega T/\omega_0)\text{.} \end{equation*}

The coefficient of the first-order term is dimensionless. We might as well simplify a bit more via

\begin{equation*} 2\zeta = \frac{b}{k\tau} = \frac{b}{\sqrt{mk}} \text{.} \end{equation*}

That way, the characteristic equation is just \(s^2+2\zeta s + 1\text{,}\) with roots \(-\zeta\pm\sqrt{\zeta^2-1}\text{.}\) Instead of having to describe what happens in terms of the original \(k,m,b\) trio, we only need worry about whether \(\zeta\) is less than, equal to, or greater than one.

Subsection 6.5.3 Diffusion equation

Let's close with the diffusion equation, also with a "forcing term" (e.g., a source of heat),

\begin{equation*} \pp{u}{t} = \alpha^2 \ppp{u}{x} + A f(x,t), \quad 0 \le x \le L\text{.} \end{equation*}

We introduce

\begin{equation*} t = \tau T, \quad x = \xi X, \quad u = \nu U\text{,} \end{equation*}

so that

\begin{equation*} \frac{\nu}{\tau} \pp{U}{T} = \frac{\alpha^2\nu }{\xi^2} \ppp{U}{X} + A f(\xi X,\tau T), \quad 0 \lt \xi X \lt L \end{equation*}

Then

\begin{equation*} \pp{U}{T} = \frac{\alpha^2\tau }{\xi^2} \ppp{U}{X} + \frac{A\tau}{\nu} F(X,T), \quad 0 \lt \xi X \lt L \end{equation*}

We have two quantities to set equal to one, but three scales at our disposal. We'll use the space scale to get rid of the size of the domain: \(\xi=L\text{,}\) so that \(0\lt X \lt 1\text{.}\) Plus,

\begin{equation*} \frac{\alpha^2\tau }{\xi^2} = 1 \quad \Rightarrow \quad \tau = \frac{L^2}{\alpha^2}, \end{equation*}
\begin{equation*} \frac{A\tau}{\nu} = 1 \quad \Rightarrow \quad \nu = \frac{AL^2}{\alpha^2}\text{.} \end{equation*}

This makes everything boil down nicely to

\begin{equation*} \pp{U}{T} = \ppp{U}{X} + F(X,T), \quad 0 \lt \xi X \lt 1\text{.} \end{equation*}
