Section 5.2 Separable equations

¶

All problems in the autonomous form \(x'=f(x)\text{,}\) and more generally in the form \(x'=f(x)g(t)\text{,}\) are called separable equations and can be solved systematically (up to performing integrations). Rather than deriving a formula for them, it's best to just repeat a straightforward process for each new problem.

Example 5.2.1.

Consider the variable growth archetype 2.1.6 \(x'=2tx\text{.}\) We express \(x'\) as \(dx/dt\) and then isolate the variables:

\begin{equation*} \frac{dx}{x} = 2t\,dt\text{.} \end{equation*}

Integrating both sides leads to \(\log |x| = t^2 + C\text{,}\) or \(|x|=Ae^{t^2}\) for a positive constant \(A\) (since it is the exponential of a real constant). Taking the absolute value off of \(x\) means that \(A\) can be negative as well. Also, \(A=0\) clearly leads to a solution. Finally, we conclude that \(x=Ce^{t^2}\) for arbitrary \(C\text{.}\)

Example 5.2.2.

Suppose \(x'=t^2/(x^3-2)\text{.}\) Separation and integration lead to

\begin{equation*} \int x^3\, dx = \int t^2\, dt\text{,} \end{equation*}

or \(\frac{1}{4}x^4 - 2x = C + \frac{1}{3}t^3\text{.}\) We could work hard to try to solve explicitly for \(y\text{,}\) but it's probably best to leave it in implicit form. This is a common limitation to separable "solutions".

Even in implicit form, we can solve for the arbitrary constant if given an initial condition. For instance, suppose \(x(0)=4\) is given for this ODE. Then \(4^4/4-2\cdot4 = C+0\text{,}\) so \(C=56\text{.}\)

Sometimes the separable structure isn't immediately apparent, and you have to manipulate the expressions a bit.

Example 5.2.3.

Suppose \(tx' = x-tx\text{.}\) Nothing happens until you see that you can factor out \(x\) on the right side. Then we have \(dx/dt = x(1-t)/t\text{,}\) or

\begin{equation*} \frac{dx}{x} = (t^{-1}-1)\,dt\text{.} \end{equation*}

Thus \(\ln|x| = \ln|t|-t+C\text{,}\) or \(x=A te^{-t}\text{.}\)

Note that this problem is also linear, so it could be approached that way as well. Of course you must get the same solution in the end!
