Section 4.6 Laplace transform methods

¶

We return to our linear, second-order, constant-coefficient problem in the simpler notation

\begin{equation*} x'' + bx' + cx = f(t)\text{.} \end{equation*}

We cast this problem as a first-order system in two dimensions, found that the eigenvalues are the roots of \(\lambda^2+b\lambda + c\text{,}\) and then constructed exponential solutions for the homogeneous part (except for the double eigenvalue case). We also found a particular solution for the case of an exponential \(f(t)\text{.}\) There is an alternative way to derive the same results using the method of Laplace transforms to systematize the manipulations.

Table 4.6.1 updates our table of Laplace transforms with additional entries. In this section and the next we examine how to use the new entries.

Subsection 4.6.1 Second derivatives

Recall that the transform of \(x'(t)\) is \(sX(s)-x(0)\text{.}\) Applying this formula twice shows that

\begin{equation*} \lx[x''(t)] = s \lx[x'(t)] - x'(0) = s^2X(s) - sx(0) - x'(0)\text{.} \end{equation*}

When we apply this to \(x''+bx'+cx=f(t)\) with constant coefficients, we obtain

\begin{equation*} [s^2X(s) - sx(0)-x'(0)] + b[sX(s)-x(0)] + cX(s) = F(s)\text{.} \end{equation*}

It is now trivial to solve for \(X(s)\text{:}\)

\begin{equation*} X(s) = \frac{ sx(0)+x'(0)}{s^2 +bs+c} + \frac{F(s)}{s^2 +bs+c}\text{.} \end{equation*}

The first fraction above is what leads to the free or unforced response, while the second is the effect of the forcing. The denominator in both cases is simply the characteristic polynomial of the ODE.

If the goal is to solve an initial-value problem with the transform, then it's necessary to find the inverse transform of \(X(s)\text{,}\) which typically involves some partial fraction efforts.

Example 4.6.2.

Let's solve \(x''-x=24 e^{3t}\text{,}\) with \(x(0)=4\text{,}\) \(x'(0)=12\text{.}\) Transforming both sides gives

\begin{equation*} [s^2X(s) - 4s - 12] - X(s) = \frac{24}{s-3}\text{.} \end{equation*}

We easily solve for \(X\text{:}\)

\begin{equation*} X(s) = \frac{4s+12}{(s^2-1)} + \frac{24}{(s^2-1)(s-3)} \text{.} \end{equation*}

In order to avoid multiple uses of partial fractions, we combine everything into a single fraction:

\begin{equation*} X(s) = \frac{4s^2-12}{(s-1)(s+1)(s-3)} \end{equation*}

The PFD takes the form

\begin{equation*} \frac{4s^2-12}{(s-1)(s+1)(s-3)} = \frac{A}{s-1} + \frac{B}{s+1} + \frac{C}{s-3}, \end{equation*}

which is the same as the identity

\begin{equation*} 4s^2-12 = A(s+1)(s-3) + B(s-1)(s-3) + C(s-1)(s+1). \end{equation*}

One way to handle this is to expand the right-hand side and equate the powers of \(s\text{.}\) In this case it's easier to choose three strategic values of \(s\text{.}\)

\begin{align*} s = 1 \amp \:\Rightarrow\: -8 = -4A \\ s = -1 \amp\: \Rightarrow\: -8 = 8B \\ s = 3 \amp\: \Rightarrow\: 24 = 8C \text{,} \end{align*}

so that

\begin{equation*} X(s) = \frac{2}{s-1} + \frac{-1}{s+1} + \frac{3}{s-3}. \end{equation*}

Now it's clear that \(x(t) = 2e^t - e^{-t} + 3e^{3t}\text{.}\)

However, if we are after a general solution in which the initial values are unknown or arbitrary, there is a way to use them to shorten the process.

Example 4.6.3.

We reconsider \(x''-x=24 e^{3t}\) with the goal of finding a general solution. As before, we obtain

\begin{equation*} X(s) = \frac{sx(0) + x'(0)}{(s-1)(s+1)} + \frac{24}{(s-1)(s+1)(s-3)}. \end{equation*}

Without manipulating this further for now, we know that we seek a PFD in the form

\begin{equation*} X(s) = \frac{A}{s-1} + \frac{B}{s+1} + \frac{C}{s-3}. \end{equation*}

Consider that this inverts to

\begin{equation*} x(t) = Ae^t + Be^{-t} + Ce^{3t}. \end{equation*}

The first two terms are due to the eigenvalues and are part of the homogeneous solution. Their coefficients are arbitrary in the general solution. It's the coefficient \(C\) that is of interest to us; i.e., a particuar solution. Suppose we chose the initial conditions in a way that zeroed out the homogeneous terms. Then

\begin{equation*} \frac{sx(0) + x'(0)}{(s-1)(s+1)} + \frac{24}{(s-1)(s+1)(s-3)} = \frac{C}{s-3}. \end{equation*}

Clearing the denominators leads to

\begin{equation*} (sx(0)+x'(0))(s-3) + 24 = C(s-1)(s+1). \end{equation*}

We don't actually care about the numerical values of the initial conditions. When we set \(s=3\text{,}\) they disappear and we get \(24=8C\text{.}\) Right away, then, we know that the general solution is

\begin{equation*} x(t) = x_h(t) + x_p(t) = c_1 e^{t} + c_2 e^{-t} + 3 e^{3t}. \end{equation*}

In light of the last example, let's look again at the general case

\begin{equation*} X(s) = \frac{ sx(0)+x'(0)}{s^2+bs+c} + \frac{F(s)}{s^2 +bs+c}\text{.} \end{equation*}

The denominators contain the characteristic polynomial, whose roots are the eigenvalues of the homogeneous problem. We refer to zeros in the denominator as poles of the transform. Each eigenvalue pole will contribute a term \(1/(s-\lambda)\) to the partial fraction decomposition. Upon inversion, this term contributes \(e^{\lambda t}\text{,}\) which is part of the homogeneous solution that we know has an arbitrary coefficient. When chasing any particular solution, we may zero those terms out when finding the decomposition.

The forcing function will contribute one or more of its own poles in \(F(s)\text{.}\) These also generate terms in the partial fraction decomposition, and these terms invert to contribute to a particular solution. The coefficients of these terms are not arbitrary and must be found from the decomposition in transform space.

Example 4.6.4.

To find the general soluton of \(x'' - 4x + 3x = 6\text{,}\) we note that the characteristic polynomial is \(s^2-4s+3=(s-3)(s-1)\text{.}\) We take transforms to obtain

\begin{equation*} X(s) = \frac{sx(0)+x'(0)}{(s-3)(s-1)} + \frac{6}{s(s-3)(s-1)} . \end{equation*}

The PFD of this has the form

\begin{equation*} X(s) = \frac{A}{s-3} + \frac{B}{(s-1)} + \frac{C}{s}. \end{equation*}

The first two terms constitute the homogeneous solution, and we zero out their coefficients and replace \(X\) with \(X_p\text{.}\) Equating to the earlier expression and clearing denominators gives

\begin{equation*} (sx(0)+x'(0))(s) + 6 = C(s-3)(s-1). \end{equation*}

We don't care about the initial values, so we choose \(s=0\) to find that \(6=3C\text{.}\) Thus \(X_p(s)=2/s\) and \(x_p(t)=2\text{.}\) Finally,

\begin{equation*} x(t) = c_1 e^{3t} + c_2 t e^{t} + 2. \end{equation*}

Subsection 4.6.2 Imaginary poles

If we transform \(e^{i\omega t}\text{,}\) we can take the real and imaginary parts to get some new formulas:

\begin{equation*} \lx[\cos(\omega t)] = \text{Re}\left( \frac{1}{s-i\omega} \frac{s+i\omega}{s+i\omega} \right) = \frac{s}{s^2+\omega^2}\text{,} \end{equation*}

and

\begin{equation*} \lx[\sin(\omega t)] = \text{Im}\left( \frac{1}{s-i\omega} \frac{s+i\omega}{s+i\omega} \right) = \frac{\omega}{s^2+\omega^2}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Conversely, a transform \(X(s)\) that has \(s^2+\omega^2\) as a factor in the denominator has poles at \(\pm i\omega\text{.}\) The simplest case is

\begin{equation*} X_p(s) = \frac{A+Bs}{s^2+\omega^2} \quad \Rightarrow \quad x_p(t)=\frac{A}{\omega}\sin(\omega t) + B\cos(\omega t)\text{.} \end{equation*}
Example 4.6.5.

Let's solve \(x''+9x=\delta(t-2)\) with \(x(0)=0\text{,}\) \(x'(0)=-3\text{.}\) Taking transforms leads to

\begin{equation*} X(s) = \frac{-3}{s^2+9} + \frac{e^{-2s}}{s^2+9} \text{.} \end{equation*}

The first term inverts easily to \(-\sin(3t)\text{.}\) The second has the form \(e^{-2s} F(s)\text{,}\) where \(F(s)\) is the transform of

\begin{equation*} f(t) = \frac{1}{3}\sin(3t)\text{.} \end{equation*}

So the shift theorem implies

\begin{equation*} x(t) = -\sin(3t) + \frac{1}{3} H(t-2) \sin(3t-6)\text{.} \end{equation*}

A pair of imaginary poles, plus one real pole, implies the PFD

\begin{equation*} X(s) = \frac{A+Bs}{s^2+\omega^2} + \frac{C}{s-r} \text{,} \end{equation*}

corresponding to \(x(t) = \frac{A}{\omega}\sin(\omega t) + B\cos(\omega t) + C e^{rt} \text{.}\) In an initial-value problem you usually have to find all three constants, whereas for the general solution we may be able to zero out the homogeneous solution.

Example 4.6.6.

To find the general soluton of \(x'' - 4x + 3x = 10\cos(t)\text{,}\) we note that the characteristic polynomial is \(s^2-4s+3=(s-3)(s-1)\text{.}\) We take transforms to obtain

\begin{equation*} X(s) = \frac{sx(0)+x'(0)}{(s-3)(s-1)} + \frac{10s}{(s^2+1)(s-3)(s-1)} . \end{equation*}

The PFD of this has the real form

\begin{equation*} X(s) = \frac{A}{s-3} + \frac{B}{s-1} + \frac{Cs+D}{s^2+1}. \end{equation*}

The first two terms constitute the homogeneous solution, and we zero out their coefficients and replace \(X\) with \(X_p\text{.}\) Equating to the earlier expression and clearing denominators gives

\begin{equation*} (sx(0)+x'(0))(s^2+1) + 10s = (Cs+D)(s-3)(s-1). \end{equation*}

We don't care about the initial values, so we evaluate both sides at \(s=i\text{:}\)

\begin{align*} 10i \amp = (Ci+D)(i-3)(i-1) = (Ci+D)(2-4i) = (4C+2D) + i(2C-4D) \text{.} \end{align*}

Equating the real and imaginary parts gives \(4C+2D=0\text{,}\) \(2C-4D=10\text{,}\) which are easily solved for \(C=1,D=-2\text{.}\) Hence our particular solution satisfies

\begin{equation*} X_p(s) = 1 \, \frac{s}{s^2+1} - 2\, \frac{1}{s^2+1}. \end{equation*}

Thus

\begin{equation*} x_p(t) = \cos(t) - 2 \sin(t), \end{equation*}

and finally

\begin{equation*} x(t) = c_1 e^{3t} + c_2 t e^{t} + \cos(t) - 2 \sin(t). \end{equation*}
Example 4.6.7.

The undamped oscillator \(x''+16x = 40e^{-2t}\) with \(x(0)=x'(0)=0\) has a solution whose transform is

\begin{equation*} X(s) = \frac{40}{(s+2)(s^2+16)} = \frac{A+Bs}{s^2+16^2} + \frac{C}{s+2}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Clearing the denominators gives the identity

\begin{equation*} (A+Bs)(s+2) + C(s^2+16) = 40\text{.} \end{equation*}

We need three equations to determine the unknown coefficients. Setting \(s=-2\) quickly gives \(20C=40\text{,}\) so \(C=2\text{.}\) Now the identity reads

\begin{equation*} Bs^2 + (A+2B)s + 2A = 8 - 2s^2. \end{equation*}

Equating the coefficients of \(1\) and \(s^2\) immediately tells us \(2A=8\) and \(B=-2\text{.}\) Hence

\begin{equation*} X(s) = \frac{4-2s}{s^2+16^2} + \frac{2}{s+2} \text{,} \end{equation*}

and finally

\begin{equation*} x_p(t) = \sin(4t) - 2\cos(4 t) + 2e^{-2t}\text{.} \end{equation*}

We may also have to deal with two sets of imaginary poles.

Example 4.6.8.

The driven, undamped oscillator \(x''+\omega_0^2 x = \cos(\omega t)\) yields

\begin{equation*} X(s) = \frac{sx(0)+x'(0)}{(s^2+\omega_0^2)} + \frac{s}{(s^2+\omega_0^2)(s^2+\omega^2)} \text{,} \end{equation*}

which has a PFD with four real constants:

\begin{equation*} X(s) = \frac{As+B}{s^2+\omega_0^2} + \frac{Cs+D}{s^2+\omega^2}. \end{equation*}

The first term is homogeneous and tells us nothing new about the general solution. So we zero out \(A,B\) for a particular solution and clear the denominators to get

\begin{equation*} (sx(0)+x'(0))(s^2+\omega^2) + s = (Cs+D)(s^2+\omega_0^2). \end{equation*}

To avoid dealing with the initial conditions, set \(s=i\omega\text{:}\)

\begin{equation*} i\omega = (iC\omega + D)(\omega_0^2-\omega^2). \end{equation*}

The real part of this equation tells us that \(D=0\text{,}\) and the imaginary part tells us \(C=1/(\omega_0^2-\omega^2)\text{.}\) Hence

\begin{equation*} x_p(t) = \frac{1}{\omega_0^2-\omega^2} \cos(\omega t). \end{equation*}

This is combined with \(\cos(\omega_0 t)\) and \(\sin(\omega_0 t)\) to get the general solution.

If all this is starting to smell like the method of undetermined coefficients to you, there's good reason. The \(x_p\) we targeted in these examples is the same one that that method produces, and the algebra is virually identical, though arrived at differently.

We do have to be aware of when the shortcut of zeroing the homogeneous terms does not apply. When delayed by the Shift Theorem, for example, those terms become geniunely different and have to be accounted for.

Example 4.6.9.

To find the general solution of \(x''+x=5H(t-4)\text{,}\) we take transforms to obtain

\begin{equation*} X(s) = \frac{sx(0)-x'(0)}{s^2+1} + \frac{5e^{-4s}}{s(s^2+1)}. \end{equation*}

The first two terms give a combination of \(\cos(t)\) and \(\sin(t)\) to contribute to the homogeneous solution. The second is in the form \(5e^{-4s}F(s)\text{,}\) where

\begin{equation*} F(s) = \frac{1}{s(s^2+1)} = \frac{A}{s} + \frac{Bs+C}{s^2+1}. \end{equation*}

We would love to discard the second PF term, but because we are going to use the shift theorem on \(f(t)\text{,}\) we have to keep them all. Clearing denominators we get

\begin{equation*} 1 = A(s^2+1) + (Bs+C)(s). \end{equation*}

Setting \(s=0\) gives us \(A=1\) right away. Then \(-s^2=Bs^2+Cs\text{,}\) which tells us that \(B=-1\) and \(C=0\text{.}\) Hence

\begin{equation*} f(t) = 1 -\cos(t). \end{equation*}

So by the shift theorem, a particular solution is

\begin{equation*} x_p(t) = 5H(t-4)f(t-4) = 5 H(t-4)[1-\cos(t-4)]. \end{equation*}

The general solution is \(c_1\cos(t) + c_2\sin(t) + x_p(t)\text{.}\)
