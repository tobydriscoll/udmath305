Section 4.6 Laplace transform methods

¶

We return to our linear, second-order, constant-coefficient problem in the simpler notation

\begin{equation*} x'' + bx' + cx = f(t)\text{.} \end{equation*}

We cast this problem as a first-order system in two dimensions, found that the eigenvalues are the roots of \(\lambda^2+b\lambda + c\text{,}\) and then constructed exponential solutions for the homogeneous part (except for the double eigenvalue case). We also found a particular solution for the case of an exponential \(f(t)\text{.}\) There is an alternative way to derive the same results using the method of Laplace transforms to systematize the manipulations.

Table 4.6.1 updates our table of Laplace transforms with additional entries. In this section we examine how to use the new entries.

Subsection 4.6.1 Second derivatives

Recall that the transform of \(x'(t)\) is \(sX(s)-x(0)\text{.}\) Applying this formula twice shows that

\begin{equation*} \lx[x''(t)] = s \lx[x'(t)] - x'(0) = s^2X(s) - sx(0) - x'(0)\text{.} \end{equation*}

When we apply this to \(x''+bx'+cx=f(t)\) with constant coefficients, we obtain

\begin{equation*} [s^2X(s) - sx(0)-x'(0)] + b[sX(s)-x(0)] + cX(s) = F(s)\text{.} \end{equation*}

It is now trivial to solve for \(X(s)\text{:}\)

\begin{equation*} X(s) = \frac{ (s+b)x(0)+x'(0)}{s^2 +bs+c} + \frac{F(s)}{s^2 +bs+c}\text{.} \end{equation*}

The first fraction above is what leads to the free or unforced response, while the second is the effect of the forcing when the initial conditions are zero. The denominator in both cases is simply the characteristic polynomial of the ODE.

If the goal is to solve an initial-value problem with the transform, then it's necessary to find the inverse transform of \(X(s)\text{,}\) which typically involves some partial fraction efforts.

Example 4.6.2.

Let's solve \(x''-x=24 e^{3t}\text{,}\) with \(x(0)=4\text{,}\) \(x'(0)=12\text{.}\) Transforming both sides gives

\begin{equation*} [s^2X(s) - 4s - 12] - X(s) = \frac{24}{s-3}\text{.} \end{equation*}

We easily solve for \(X\text{:}\)

\begin{equation*} X(s) = \frac{4s+12}{(s^2-1)} + \frac{24}{(s^2-1)(s-3)} \text{.} \end{equation*}

In order to avoid multiple uses of partial fractions, we combine everything into a single fraction:

\begin{equation*} X(s) = \frac{4s^2-12}{(s-1)(s+1)(s-3)} \end{equation*}

The PFD takes the form

\begin{equation*} \frac{4s^2-12}{(s-1)(s+1)(s-3)} = \frac{A}{s-1} + \frac{B}{s+1} + \frac{C}{s-3}, \end{equation*}

which is the same as the identity

\begin{equation*} 4s^2-12 = A(s+1)(s-3) + B(s-1)(s-3) + C(s-1)(s+1). \end{equation*}

One way to handle this is to expand the right-hand side and equate the powers of \(s\text{.}\) In this case it's easier to choose three strategic values of \(s\text{.}\)

\begin{align*} s = 1 \amp \:\Rightarrow\: -8 = -4A \\ s = -1 \amp\: \Rightarrow\: -8 = 8B \\ s = 3 \amp\: \Rightarrow\: 24 = 8C \text{,} \end{align*}

so that

\begin{equation*} X(s) = \frac{2}{s-1} + \frac{-1}{s+1} + \frac{3}{s-3}. \end{equation*}

Now it's clear that \(x(t) = 2e^t - e^{-t} + 3e^{3t}\text{.}\)

In light of the last example, let's look again at the general case

\begin{equation*} X(s) = \frac{ (s+b)x(0)+x'(0)}{s^2+bs+c} + \frac{F(s)}{s^2 +bs+c}\text{.} \end{equation*}

The denominators contain the characteristic polynomial, whose roots are the eigenvalues of the homogeneous problem. We refer to zeros in the denominator as poles of the transform. Each eigenvalue pole will contribute a term \(1/(s-\lambda)\) to the partial fraction decomposition. Upon inversion, this term contributes \(e^{\lambda t}\text{,}\) which is part of the homogeneous solution. The forcing function will contribute one or more of its own poles in \(F(s)\text{,}\) and they generate new terms in the partial fraction decomposition.

Example 4.6.3.

To solve \(x'' - 4x + 3x = 6\) with \(x(0)=-2\text{,}\) \(x'(0)=4\text{,}\) we take transforms to obtain

\begin{equation*} X(s) = \frac{-2s + 8 + 4}{(s-3)(s-1)} + \frac{6}{s(s-3)(s-1)} . \end{equation*}

The PFD of this has the form

\begin{equation*} X(s) = \frac{A}{s-3} + \frac{B}{s-1} + \frac{C}{s}. \end{equation*}

Equating and clearing the denominators leads to

\begin{equation*} (s)(12-2s) + 6 = As(s-1) + Bs(s-3) + C(s-1)(s-3). \end{equation*}

The values \(s=0,s=1,s=3\text{,}\) which are simply the poles of \(X\text{,}\) leave especially simple equations: \(6=3C\text{,}\) \(10+6=-2B\text{,}\) and \(18+6=6A\text{.}\) Hence

\begin{equation*} X(s) = 4\, \frac{1}{s-3} - 8\, \frac{1}{s-1} + 2\, \frac{1}{s}. \end{equation*}

This inverts easily to give \(x(t) = 4e^{3t} - 8e^t + 2\text{.}\)

Subsection 4.6.2 Imaginary poles

If we transform \(e^{i\omega t}\text{,}\) we can take the real and imaginary parts to get some new formulas:

\begin{equation*} \lx[\cos(\omega t)] = \text{Re}\left( \frac{1}{s-i\omega} \frac{s+i\omega}{s+i\omega} \right) = \frac{s}{s^2+\omega^2}\text{,} \end{equation*}

and

\begin{equation*} \lx[\sin(\omega t)] = \text{Im}\left( \frac{1}{s-i\omega} \frac{s+i\omega}{s+i\omega} \right) = \frac{\omega}{s^2+\omega^2}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Conversely, a transform \(X(s)\) that has \(s^2+\omega^2\) as a factor in the denominator has poles at \(\pm i\omega\text{.}\) The simplest case is

\begin{equation*} X(s) = \frac{A+Bs}{s^2+\omega^2} \quad \Rightarrow \quad x(t)=\frac{A}{\omega}\sin(\omega t) + B\cos(\omega t)\text{.} \end{equation*}
Example 4.6.4.

Let's solve \(x''+9x=\delta(t-2)\) with \(x(0)=0\text{,}\) \(x'(0)=-3\text{.}\) Taking transforms leads to

\begin{equation*} X(s) = \frac{-3}{s^2+9} + \frac{e^{-2s}}{s^2+9} \text{.} \end{equation*}

The first term inverts easily to \(-\sin(3t)\text{.}\) The second has the form \(e^{-2s} F(s)\text{,}\) where \(F(s)\) is the transform of

\begin{equation*} f(t) = \frac{1}{3}\sin(3t)\text{.} \end{equation*}

So the shift theorem implies

\begin{equation*} x(t) = -\sin(3t) + \frac{1}{3} H(t-2) \sin(3t-6)\text{.} \end{equation*}
Example 4.6.5.

To find the general soluton of \(x'' - 4x' + 3x = 10\cos(t)\) with zero initial conditions, we take transforms to obtain

\begin{equation*} X(s) = \frac{10s}{(s^2+1)(s-3)(s-1)} . \end{equation*}

The PFD of this has the real form

\begin{equation*} X(s) = \frac{A}{s-3} + \frac{B}{s-1} + \frac{Cs+D}{s^2+1}. \end{equation*}

Equating and clearing denominators gives

\begin{equation*} 10s = A(s-1)(s^2+1) + B(s-3)(s^2+1) + (Cs+D)(s-3)(s-1). \end{equation*}

We could equate powers of \(s\) to get four linear equations for the constants. However, life is again easier if we evaluate the identity at the poles. We don't care about the initial values, so we evaluate both sides at \(s=i\text{:}\)

\begin{align*} s=1 \amp \:\Rightarrow\: 10 = -4B \\ s=3 \amp \:\Rightarrow\: 30 = 20A \\ s=i \amp \:\Rightarrow\: 10i = (i-1)(i-3)(iC+D) = (2-4i)(iC+D) = (4C+2D) + i(2C-4D) \text{.} \end{align*}

Equating the real and imaginary parts in the last line gives \(4C+2D=0\text{,}\) \(2C-4D=10\text{,}\) which are easily solved for \(C=1,D=-2\text{.}\) Hence

\begin{equation*} X(s) = \frac{3/2}{s-3} + \frac{-5/2}{s-1} + \frac{s-2}{s^2+1}, \end{equation*}

and finally

\begin{equation*} x(t) = \frac{3}{2} e^{3t} -\frac{5}{2} e^{t} + \cos(t) - 2 \sin(t). \end{equation*}
Example 4.6.6.

The undamped oscillator \(x''+16x = 40e^{-2t}\) with \(x(0)=x'(0)=0\) has a solution whose transform is

\begin{equation*} X(s) = \frac{40}{(s+2)(s^2+16)} = \frac{A+Bs}{s^2+16^2} + \frac{C}{s+2}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Clearing the denominators gives the identity

\begin{equation*} (A+Bs)(s+2) + C(s^2+16) = 40\text{.} \end{equation*}

We need three equations to determine the unknown coefficients. Setting \(s=-2\) quickly gives \(20C=40\text{,}\) so \(C=2\text{.}\) Now the identity reads

\begin{equation*} Bs^2 + (A+2B)s + 2A = 8 - 2s^2. \end{equation*}

Equating the coefficients of \(1\) and \(s^2\) immediately tells us \(2A=8\) and \(B=-2\text{.}\) Hence

\begin{equation*} X(s) = \frac{4-2s}{s^2+16^2} + \frac{2}{s+2} \text{,} \end{equation*}

and finally

\begin{equation*} x_p(t) = \sin(4t) - 2\cos(4 t) + 2e^{-2t}\text{.} \end{equation*}
Example 4.6.7.

To find the general solution of \(x''+x=5H(t-4)\) starting from rest, we take transforms to obtain

\begin{equation*} X(s) = \frac{5e^{-4s}}{s(s^2+1)} = 5e^{-4s}F(s) \text{,} \end{equation*}

where

\begin{equation*} F(s) = \frac{1}{s(s^2+1)} = \frac{A}{s} + \frac{Bs+C}{s^2+1}. \end{equation*}

Clearing denominators we get

\begin{equation*} 1 = A(s^2+1) + (Bs+C)(s). \end{equation*}

Setting \(s=0\) gives us \(A=1\) right away. Then \(-s^2=Bs^2+Cs\text{,}\) which tells us that \(B=-1\) and \(C=0\text{.}\) Hence

\begin{equation*} f(t) = 1 -\cos(t). \end{equation*}

So by the shift theorem, the solution is

\begin{equation*} x(t) = 5H(t-4)f(t-4) = 5 H(t-4)[1-\cos(t-4)]. \end{equation*}

Subsection 4.6.3 Complex poles

Complex poles in the transform correspond to complex exponentials in the time domain. When they occur in conjugate pairs, the entire expression can be cast in real terms. It can help to use the transform-shift formula

\begin{equation*} \lx[e^{at}f(t)] = F(s-a)\text{.} \end{equation*}

We should have enough information from the ODE to know what to choose as the real shift \(a\text{.}\)

Example 4.6.8.

Suppose \(x''+2x'+10x=6\delta(t-1)\text{,}\) with zero initial conditions. Then

\begin{equation*} X(s) = \frac{6e^{-s}}{s^2+2s+10}\text{.} \end{equation*}

The quadratic formula tells us the homogeneous eigenvalues \(-1\pm 3i\text{,}\) which will contribute an amplitude \(e^{-t}\) in time. This corresponds to a shift \(a=-1\text{,}\) so we designate \(X(s)=F(s+1)\text{.}\) Thus

\begin{equation*} F(s) = X(s-1) = 6e \frac{e^{-s}}{s^2-2s+1+2s-2+10} = 6e \frac{e^{-s}}{s^2+9}. \end{equation*}

Now we can apply the Shift Theorem to \(F\) using the definition \(V(s)=1/(s^2+9)\) and get

\begin{equation*} f(t) = 6e H(t-1) v(t-1), \end{equation*}

where \(v(t)=\frac{1}{3}\sin(3t)\text{.}\) When we unwind the definitions, we get

\begin{equation*} x(t) = e^{-t} f(t) = 2 e^{-t+1} H(t-1)\sin(3t-3). \end{equation*}

The drawback to Laplace transforms as a one-stop solver is what we observed earlier: the particular solution we find virtually always includes some terms from the homogeneous solution. Mathematically, the poles of \(X_p(s)\) include those of the transfer function and the forcing function. This combination makes the partial fraction decompositions lengthier, to say the least. Undetermined coefficients is a less sexy but simpler way to solve concrete problems in most cases, especially if one seeks a general solution without initial conditions to consider at the end.

Subsection 4.6.4 Repeated poles and resonance

Repeated poles in the Laplace transform of a solution appear in the form of denominator terms such as \((s-a)^k\) or \((s^2+\omega^2)^k\) with \(k \gt 1\text{.}\) They can arise in two ways: from repeated eigenvalues in the homogeneous ODE (e.g., critical damping), or because the forcing function has a pole in common with one of the eigenvalues (e.g., perfect resonance).

Consider inverting the double real pole

\begin{equation*} X(s) = \frac{1}{(s-a)^2}\text{.} \end{equation*}

The key is to note that \(X(s)=-F'(s)\text{,}\) where \(F(s) = (s-a)^{-1}\) is the transform of \(e^{at}\text{.}\) From the transform definition we compute

\begin{align*} -F'(s) \amp = -\frac{d}{ds} \int_0^\infty f(t) e^{-s t}\, dt\\ \amp = \int_0^\infty t f(t) e^{-s t}\, dt\\ \amp = \lx[ t f(t) ] = \lx[ t e^{at} ] \text{.} \end{align*}

If we keep differentiating in \(s\) we find

\begin{equation} \lx[t^n e^{at} ] = \frac{n!}{(s-a)^{n+1}}.\label{so-eq-repeatedexp}\tag{4.6.1} \end{equation}

The special case \(a=0\) allows us to transform any power of \(t\text{,}\) and therefore any polynomial.

Example 4.6.9.

The critically damped oscillator \(x'' + 6x' + 9=0\) has a double eigenvalue at \(-3\text{.}\) To find the solution with \(x(0)=0\text{,}\) \(x'(0)=6\text{,}\) we use the transform to find

\begin{equation*} [s^2X(s) - 6] + 6[sX(s)] + 9[X(s)] = 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad X(s) = \frac{6}{(s+3)^2}. \end{equation*}

Hence (4.6.1) applies with \(n=1\text{,}\) and

\begin{equation*} x(t) = 6t e^{-3t}\text{.} \end{equation*}

If you look carefully at the derivation of (4.6.1), you will see that the identity of \(f(t)\) played no role in the key steps. As shown in Table 4.6.1, we can repeat the calculation for a generic \(F(s)\text{:}\)

\begin{equation*} \lx[t^n f(t) ] = (-1)^n F^{(n)}(s), \qquad n=1,2,3,\dots. \end{equation*}

Take a moment to appreciate this. Earlier we found that a derivative in \(t\) essentially corresponds to multiplying by \(s\text{.}\) Up to a change in sign, a derivative in \(s\) corresponds to multiplying by \(t\text{!}\)

Example 4.6.10.

The undamped oscillator \(x''+9x=\sin(3t)\) has a perfect resonance. From zero initial conditions we have

\begin{equation*} X(s) = \frac{3}{(s^2+9)^2}\text{.} \end{equation*}

We are going to use

\begin{equation*} \frac{d}{ds} \left[ \frac{3}{s^2+9} \right] = \frac{-6s}{(s^2+9)^2}. \end{equation*}

Specifically, if we designate \(G(s)=2sX(s)\text{,}\) then we know that

\begin{equation*} \lx[ t \sin(3t) ] = -\frac{d}{ds} \left[ \frac{3}{s^2+9} \right] = G(s), \end{equation*}

and therefore \(g(t)=t\sin(3t)\text{.}\) Next, note that

\begin{equation*} 2\lx[x'(t)] = 2[sX(s)-X(0)] = G(s). \end{equation*}

Hence \(x'(t) = \frac{1}{2} t \sin(3t)\text{,}\) and integration by parts yields

\begin{equation*} x(t) = \frac{1}{18} [ \sin(3t) - 3t\cos(3t) ]\text{.} \end{equation*}

Exercises 4.6.5 Challenge problems for 2nd order Laplace

¶
1. Exp-harmonic case.

Show that

\begin{equation*} \lx\left[ e^{at}\cos(\omega t) \right] = \frac{s-a}{(s-a)^2+\omega^2}, \qquad \lx\left[ e^{at}\sin(\omega t) \right] = \frac{\omega}{(s-a)^2+\omega^2}. \end{equation*}

(These can be used directly in place of the shift-then-invert strategy in the examples above.)

2. Imaginary PFD.

Show that by doing a partial fraction decomposition over linear factors with imaginary poles,

\begin{equation} \lx^{-1}\left[\dfrac{As+B}{s^2+\omega^2}\right] = A\cos(\omega t) + \dfrac{B}{\omega}\sin(\omega t)\text{,}\label{so-eq-laplace-imag}\tag{4.6.2} \end{equation}

if \(\omega \neq 0\text{.}\) (Of course in practice the transform table tells you this directly.)

Hint

Start with

\begin{equation*} \dfrac{As+B}{s^2+\omega^2} = \frac{C}{s-i\omega} + \frac{D}{s+i\omega} \end{equation*}

You may not assume that \(C,D\) are real, but they are conjugates of one another.

3. Sneaking up on the origin.

Show how taking \(\omega\to 0\) in the result (4.6.2) can be used to get the inverse transform of \(\dfrac{1}{s^2}\text{.}\)
