Section 1.3 Matrix algebra

¶

Subsection 1.3.1 Matrix multiplication

Addition and scalar multiplication of matrices is exactly the same as for vectors—elementwise. Things get interesting when we get to matrix products.

In order to define the product \(\bA\bB\text{,}\) we require that the number of columns in \(\bA\) is the same as the number of rows in \(\bB\text{.}\) We say that the inner dimensions must agree; otherwise, the product is considered undefined.

Definition 1.3.1. Matrix times matrix.

If \(\bA\) is \(m\times n\) and \(\bB\) is \(n\times p\text{,}\) then the product \(\bA\bB\) is defined as

\begin{equation} \bA\mathbf{B} = \bA \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{b}_1 \amp \mathbf{b}_2 \amp \cdots \amp \mathbf{b}_p \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \bA\mathbf{b}_1 \amp \bA\mathbf{b}_2 \amp \cdots \amp \bA\mathbf{b}_p \end{bmatrix}\text{.}\label{la-eq-matmat}\tag{1.3.1} \end{equation}

In words, a matrix-matrix product is the horizontal concatenation of matrix-vector products involving the columns of the right-hand matrix. Note that when we multiply \(m\times n\) by \(n\times p\text{,}\) the result is \(m\times p\text{;}\) that is, the outer dimensions remain.

When we compute a matrix product by hand, we usually don't write out the above. Instead we use a more compact definition for the individual entries of \(\bC = \bA\bB\text{,}\)

\begin{equation} C_{ij} = \sum_{k=1}^n A_{ik}B_{kj}, \qquad i=1,\ldots,m, \quad j=1,\ldots,p\text{.}\label{la-eq-matmatinner}\tag{1.3.2} \end{equation}
Example 1.3.2.

Let

\begin{equation*} \bA = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp -1 \\ 0 \amp 2 \\ -3 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad \mathbf{B} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \amp -1 \amp 0 \amp 4 \\ 1 \amp 1 \amp 3 \amp 2 \end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Then

\begin{align*} \bA\mathbf{B} \amp= \begin{bmatrix} (1)(2) + (-1)(1) \amp (1)(-1) + (-1)(1) \amp (1)(0) + (-1)(3) \amp (1)(4) + (-1)(2) \\ (0)(2) + (2)(1) \amp (0)(-1) + (2)(1) \amp (0)(0) + (2)(3) \amp (0)(4) + (2)(2) \\ (-3)(2) + (1)(1) \amp (-3)(-1) + (1)(1) \amp (-3)(0) + (1)(3) \amp (-3)(4) + (1)(2) \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp -2 \amp -3 \amp 2 \\ 2 \amp 2 \amp 6 \amp 4 \\ -5 \amp 4 \amp 3 \amp -10 \end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{align*}

Observe that

\begin{equation*} \bA \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} = 2 \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ -3 \end{bmatrix} + 1 \begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ 2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ -5 \end{bmatrix}\text{,} \end{equation*}

and so on.

Subsection 1.3.2 Properties

First, the bad news. In the previous subsection we computed the product \(\bA\bB\text{,}\) where the matrices are \(3\times 2\) and \(2\times 4\text{,}\) to get a result that is \(3\times 4\text{.}\) However, the product \(\bB\bA\) isn't even defined, because \(4\neq 3\text{.}\) Moreover, even when both \(\bA\bB\) and \(\bB\bA\) are defined, we cannot automatically expect them to be equal. You may have to unlearn some very old habits.

In a product of matrices (and vectors), position matters. You cannot change the ordering without an explicit justification!

Fact 1.3.3.

Matrix multiplication is not commutative. That is, the order of terms in a product affects the result.

Fortunately, other familiar properties of multiplication do come along for the ride:

  1. \(\displaystyle (\bA\bB)\bC=\bA(\bB\bC)\)
  2. \(\displaystyle \bA(\bB+\bC) = \bA\bB + \bA\bC\)
  3. \(\displaystyle (\bA+\bB)\bC = \bA\bC + \bB\bC\)

Subsection 1.3.3 Identity matrix

It's pretty immediate that for any square matrix \(\bA\text{,}\) \(\bA\boldsymbol{0}=\boldsymbol{0}\bA=\boldsymbol{0}\) for the all-zero square matrix \(\boldsymbol{0}\) of approrpiate size. So we have a multiplicative zero. But the situation is a bit less obvious when it comes to a multiplicative unit element (the counterpart of the scalar 1).

Definition 1.3.4. Identity matrix.

The \(n\times n\) identity matrix is

\begin{equation*} \bI = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp \cdots \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 1 \amp 0 \amp \cdots \amp 0 \\ \vdots \amp \amp \ddots \amp \amp \vdots\\ 0 \amp \cdots \amp 0 \amp 1 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp \cdots \amp 0 \amp 0 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}

The definition of the identity is such that \(\bA\bI=\bA\) for all \(\bA\in\Rmn{m}{n}\) and \(\bI\bB=\bB\) for all \(\bB\in\Rmn{n}{p}\text{.}\) This includes vectors, i.e., \(\bI\bx=\bx\) for all \(\bx \in \Rn{n}\text{.}\)
