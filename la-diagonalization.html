Section 1.11 Diagonalization

¶

Say \(\bA\) is \(n\times n\text{,}\) and that we write

\begin{equation*} \bA \bv_1 = \lambda_1 \bv_1, \; \bA \bv_2 = \lambda_2 \bv_2,\; \dots, \; \bA \bv_n = \lambda_n \bv_n\text{,} \end{equation*}

where \(\lambda_1,\lambda_2,\dots,\lambda_n\) are its eigenvalues (including multiplicities). Through some linear algebra magic, we can express this collection of equations more succinctly:

\begin{align*} \begin{bmatrix} \bA \bv_1 \amp \bA \bv_2 \amp \cdots \amp \bA \bv_n \end{bmatrix} \amp = \begin{bmatrix} \lambda_1\bv_1 \amp \lambda_2\bv_2 \amp \cdots \amp \lambda_n\bv_n \end{bmatrix}\\ \bA \begin{bmatrix} \bv_1 \amp \bv_2 \amp \cdots \amp \bv_n \end{bmatrix} \amp = \begin{bmatrix} \bv_1 \amp \bv_2 \amp \cdots \amp \bv_n \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \lambda_1 \amp 0 \amp \cdots \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp \lambda_2 \amp \cdots \amp 0 \\ \amp \amp \ddots \amp \\ 0 \amp \cdots \amp 0 \amp \lambda_n \end{bmatrix}\\ \bA \bV \amp = \bV \bD \text{,} \end{align*}

where \(\bD\) is a diagonal matrix.

If \(\bV\) is nonsingular, we therefore can write

\begin{equation} \bA = \bV \bD \bV^{-1}\text{,}\label{la-eq-diagonalization}\tag{1.11.1} \end{equation}

which is called a diagonalization of \(\bA\text{.}\) A diagonalization is not unique; after all, we can take the eigenvalues in any order, and the eigenvectors themselves are not unique.

When a matrix has a diagonalization, the two-output form of the eig MATLAB command gives the matrices V and D for one.

Example 1.11.1. A 2-by-2 diagonalization.

Let's find a diagonalization of

\begin{equation*} \mathbf{A} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 1 \\ 4 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}

We found the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of this guy in the previous section. We got \(\lambda_1=3\) with \(\bv_1 = \twovec{1}{2}\text{,}\) and \(\lambda_2=-1\) with \(\bv_2 =\twovec{1}{-2}\text{.}\) So we paint by numbers to get

\begin{equation*} \mathbf{D} = \begin{bmatrix} 3 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp -1 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad \bV = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 1 \\ 2 \amp -2 \end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}

We "just" need to invert \(\bV\text{;}\) fortunately, we're only at \(2\times 2\text{,}\) so this is a snap.

\begin{equation*} \bV^{-1} = \frac{1}{-4} \begin{bmatrix} -2 \amp -1 \\ -2 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1/2 \amp 1/4 \\ 1/2 \amp -1/4 \end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Hence

\begin{equation*} \mathbf{A} = \bV \mathbf{D} \bV^{-1} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 1 \\ 2 \amp -2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 3 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp -1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1/2 \amp 1/4 \\ 1/2 \amp -1/4 \end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Not every matrix has a diagonalization. The essential issue is whether \(\bV\) is invertible. We already have the condition needed to ensure this.

Theorem 1.11.2. Diagonalizability.

A matrix is diagonalizable if and only if it has no defective eigenvalues.

If we put that together with Theorem 1.10.16, then we can also conclude that a matrix whose eigenvalues are all simple (multiplicity one) is diagonalizable. Any repeated eigenvalues might or might not be defective, and investigation of the eigenspace is required in order to determine which. In the \(2\times 2\) case, though, we can make a simpler determination.

Fact 1.11.3.

A \(2\times 2\) matrix with repeated eigenvalue is either a multiple of the identity, in which case it is already diagonal, or it is defective.

Subsection 1.11.1 Eigenvector basis (optional)

¶

Equation (1.11.1) probably looks quite strange. It was derived by writing out all of the eigenpair equations simulataneously, and we will see that it has some uses later on. But what is it about?

Focus first on the inner diagonal matrix \(\bD\text{.}\) Diagonal matrices are about as simple as matrices get: for any vector \(\bu\text{,}\)

\begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} \lambda_1 \amp \amp \amp \\ \amp \lambda_2 \amp \amp \\ \amp \amp \ddots \amp \\ \amp \amp \amp \lambda_n \end{bmatrix} \bu = \begin{bmatrix} \lambda_1 u_1 \\ \lambda_2 u_2 \\ \vdots \\ \lambda_n u_n \end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Notice how in the diagonal case all the vector components are completely decouplede. Now, given a vector \(\bx\text{,}\) define \(\bu=\bV^{-1}\bx\text{.}\) Then clearly

\begin{equation*} \bx = \bV\bu = u_1 \bv_1 +\cdots + u_n \bv_n\text{.} \end{equation*}

In words, the entries of \(\bu\) are the coefficients for expressing \(\bx\) as a linear combination of eigenvectors. We call these the coordinates of \(\bx\) in an eigenvector basis (consider Theorem 1.8.1).

Let's put all this together:

\begin{equation*} \bA \bx = (\bV \bD \bV^{-1})\bx = \bV \bD (\bV^{-1}\bx) \text{,} \end{equation*}

which implies that

\begin{equation*} \bV^{-1} ( \bA\bx) = \bD (\bV^{-1}\bx) \text{,} \end{equation*}

which states that the action of \(\bA\) on any vector is diagonal when cast into eigenvector coordinates.
