Section 6.3 Diffusion equation

¶

Imagine a long, narrow subway car. All the seats are taken, and some people are standing in the aisle. Then at a stop, a lot of new people come into the car through several door locations. Immediately the areas near the doors become packed with people. The people at the fringes of these knots move away from them and into less densely packed space, and the people behind them follow suit. Ideally, eventually the aisle ends up more or less uniformly dense everywhere.

How could we model this situation? First, imagine that there are lots of passengers–billions–so that we can use a continuous variable to describe them. Namely, we use the density of passengers per unit length, which we call \(\rho\text{.}\) This quantity depends on both space \(x\) and time \(t\text{.}\) Now let us choose a particular position and time and look at a small segment of the aisle of length \(\Delta x\text{.}\) If we assume that passengers are neither created nor destroyed, then

\begin{equation*} \pp{}{t} (\rho(t,x) \Delta x) \approx Q(t,x) - Q(t,x+\Delta x), \end{equation*}

where \(Q\) is the flux, or net rate of movement of passengers. In the limit this becomes

\begin{equation*} \pp{\rho}{t} + \pp{Q}{x} = 0, \end{equation*}

which is the statement of a conservation law. To complete the model we need a law relating \(Q\) back to the density \(\rho\text{.}\) A fundamentally important choice is Fick's Law, which says

\begin{equation*} Q = -\kappa \pp{\rho}{x} \end{equation*}

for a diffusivity \(\kappa>0\text{.}\) The law posits that people move in the direction of less density, and at a speed proportional to the density gradient. Altogether we get

\begin{equation*} \pp{\rho}{t} = \kappa \ppp{\rho}{x}, \end{equation*}

which is known as the diffusion equation or the heat equation. It has many applications, being the archetype of all diffusive phenomena: cream in coffee, animals in a habitat, rumors on a social network, and heat in a rod.

This is our first partial differential equation. There are two independent variables now, \(x\) and \(t\text{.}\) Like our earlier ODEs in time, it allows an initial condition,

\begin{equation*} \rho(0,x) = f(x)\text{,} \end{equation*}

and like our ODEs in space, two boundary conditions,

\begin{equation*} \rho(t,0) = \rho(t,\pi) = 0\text{.} \end{equation*}

(Aren't all subway cars exactly \(\pi\) long?) Conditions like these on the value of the dependent variable at the boundary are called Dirichlet conditions. Conditions that are instead on the first spatial derivative,

\begin{equation*} \pp{\rho}{x}(t,0) = \pp{\rho}{x}(t,\pi) = 0\text{,} \end{equation*}

are called Neumann conditions. They are restrictions on the flux (which is much more realistic for our original subway car example).
