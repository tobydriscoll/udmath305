Section 1.10 Separable equations

¶

Refer to section 1.8 of the text (pp. 65-66).

We saw in Section 1.9 that problems in the form \(y'=f(y)\) may be solvable by separating the variables. In fact the same trick works for problems of the more general form \(y'=f(y)g(t)\text{.}\) These are called separable equations. Rather than deriving a formula for them, we just repeat the process for each new problem.

Example 1.10.1

Consider the variable growth archetype 1.1.3 \(y'=2ty\text{.}\) We express \(y'\) as \(dy/dt\) and then isolate the variables:

\begin{equation*} \frac{dy}{y} = 2t\,dt\text{.} \end{equation*}

Integrating both sides leads to \(\log |y| = t^2 + C\text{,}\) or \(|y|=Ae^{t^2}\) for a positive constant \(A\) (since it is the exponential of a real constant). Taking the absolute value off of \(y\) means that \(A\) can be negative as well. Also, \(A=0\) clearly leads to a solution. Finally, then, \(y=Ce^{t^2}\) for arbitrary \(C\text{.}\)

Example 1.10.2

Suppose \(y'=t^2/(y^3-2)\text{.}\) Separation and integration lead to

\begin{equation*} \int y^3\, dy = \int t^2\, dt\text{,} \end{equation*}

or \(\frac{1}{4}y^4 - 2y = C + \frac{1}{3}t^3\text{.}\) We could work hard to try to solve explicitly for \(y\text{,}\) but it's probably best to leave it in implicit form. This is a common limitation to separable "solutions".

Even in implicit form, we can solve for the arbitrary constant if given an initial condition. For instance, suppose \(y(0)=4\) is given for this ODE. Then \(4^4/4-2\cdot4 = C+0\text{,}\) so \(C=56\text{.}\)

Sometimes the separable structure isn't immediately apparent, and you have to manipulate the expressions a bit.

Example 1.10.3

Suppose \(ty' = y-ty\text{.}\) Nothing happens until you see that you can factor out \(y\) on the right side. Then we have \(dy/dt = y(1-t)/t\text{,}\) or

\begin{equation*} \frac{dy}{y} = (t^{-1}-1)\,dt\text{.} \end{equation*}

Thus \(\ln|y| = \ln|t|-t+C\text{,}\) or \(y=A te^{-t}\text{.}\)

Note that this problem is also linear, so it could be approached that way as well. Of course you must get the same solution in the end!
