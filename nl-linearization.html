Section 5.5 Linearization

¶

A pendulum with length \(L\) and angle of deflection \(\theta(t)\) from the vertical is governed by the nonlinear second-order equation

\begin{equation*} \ddd{\theta}{t} + \frac{g}{L} \sin(\theta) = 0\text{,} \end{equation*}

where \(g\) is gravitational acceleration. It's standard to argue that as long as \(|\theta|\) remains small, a good approximation is the linear problem

\begin{equation*} \ddd{\theta}{t} + \frac{g}{L} \theta = 0\text{,} \end{equation*}

because \(\sin \theta \approx \theta\text{.}\) We want to get more systematic with this process.

First note that we can recast the nonlinear problem as a first-order system in two variables. Define \(x=\theta\) and \(y=\theta'\text{.}\) Then

\begin{align*} \dd{x}{t} \amp = y \\ \dd{y}{t} \amp = -\frac{g}{L}\sin(x)\text{.} \end{align*}

This trick works for any nonlinear second-order equation \(\theta''=g(t,\theta,\theta')\text{.}\) Thus we can focus on problems in the general form

\begin{align*} \dd{x}{t} \amp = F(x,y) \\ \dd{y}{t} \amp = G(x,y)\text{,} \end{align*}

or \(\bu' = \bff(\bu)\) for 2-vector \(\bu(t)\text{.}\) Note that (as we saw before), the vector \(\bu\) represents the state of the system.

As we did with single scalar equations, we will pay close attention to steady states or fixed points of these systems. Here this means constants \((x_p,y_p)\) such that \(F(x_p,y_p)=G(x_p,y_p)=0\text{.}\) For the nonlinear pendulum, both \((0,0)\) and \((\pi,0)\) are steady states.

We are interested in the stability of fixed points, that is, the dynamics close to them. We will use linear approximations of the functions \(F\) and \(G\) near a fixed point:

\begin{align*} F(x,y) \amp \approx F(x_p,y_p) + \pp{F}{x} (x-x_p) + \pp{F}{y} (y-y_p)\\ G(x,y) \amp \approx G(x_p,y_p) + \pp{G}{x} (x-x_p) + \pp{G}{y} (y-y_p)\text{,} \end{align*}

where it is understood for brevity that the partial derivatives are all evaluated at \((x_p,y_p)\text{.}\) Given that \((x_p,y_p)\) is a fixed point, two terms above are zero. Finally, define \(u_1(t)=x(t)-x_p\text{,}\) \(u_2(t)=y(t)-y_p\) to arrive at

\begin{align*} \dd{u_1}{t} \amp \approx \pp{F}{x} u_1 + \pp{F}{y} u_2, \\ \dd{u_2}{t} \amp \approx \pp{G}{x} u_1 + \pp{G}{y} u_2. \end{align*}

This inspires the following definition.

Definition 5.5.1. Jacobian matrix.

The Jacobian matrix of the system \(x'=F(x,y),\, y'=G(x,y)\) is

\begin{equation} \bJ(x,y) = \twomat{\pp{F}{x}}{\pp{F}{y}}{\pp{G}{x}}{\pp{G}{y}}\label{nl-eq-jacobian}\tag{5.5.1} \end{equation}

Let's summarize. In the neighborhood of a fixed point \((x_p,y_p)\text{,}\) we can define the “deviation from equilibrium” as the variables \(u_1(t)=x(t)-x_p\text{,}\) \(u_2(t)=y(t)-y_p\text{.}\) These variables approximately satisfy \(\bu'=\bJ(x_p,y_p)\bu\text{,}\) which is a linear, constant-coefficient system in two dimensions. This is a huge revelation: near a steady state, dynamics are mostly linear.

We are now right back into the situation of Section 3.5. In particular:

Fact 5.5.2.

The stability of a steady state is (usually) determined by the eigenvalues of the Jacobian matrix at the steady state.

Example 5.5.3. Linearization of the pendulum.

Let's examine the steady states of a pendulum with damping,

\begin{equation*} \ddd{\theta}{t} + \gamma \theta' + \frac{g}{L} \sin(\theta) = 0\text{,} \end{equation*}

with \(\gamma \gt 0\text{.}\) It transforms into the first-order system

\begin{align*} \dd{x}{t} \amp = y \\ \dd{y}{t} \amp = -\frac{g}{L}\sin(x) - \gamma y\text{.} \end{align*}

These define \(F(x,y)\) and \(G(x,y)\text{.}\) From here we note all the first partial derivatives:

\begin{align*} \pp{F}{x} = 0 \amp \qquad \pp{F}{y} = 1\\ \pp{G}{x} = - \frac{g}{L}\cos(x) \amp \qquad \pp{G}{y} = -\gamma\text{.} \end{align*}

The first steady state we consider is at the origin. Here the Jacobian matrix is

\begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} 0 \amp 1 \\ -g/L \amp -\gamma \end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}

We get \(T=-\gamma \lt 0\) and \(D=g/L \gt 0\text{,}\) which is a stable sink. That comports with our experience with a pendulum hanging straight down!

The other steady state is at \((\pi,0)\text{.}\) Now the Jacobian is

\begin{equation*} \begin{bmatrix} 0 \amp 1 \\ g/L \amp -\gamma \end{bmatrix}\text{,} \end{equation*}

with \(T=-\gamma\) and \(D=-g/L\text{.}\) This implies a saddle point, so the "inverted pendulum" is indeed unstable.

The caveat on using eigenvalues for stability is when they both have zero real part, which is neutrally or weakly stable in the linear sense. In such cases the details of the nonlinear terms of the system can swing the stability either way.

Example 5.5.4. Linearization of predator-prey.

The system

\begin{align*} \frac{dx}{dt} \amp= 3x-\frac{xy}{2}\\ \frac{dy}{dt} \amp= -y+\frac{xy}{4} \end{align*}

is called a predator–prey equation. If species \(y\) is set to zero, species \(x\) would grow exponentially on its own (the prey). Similarly, species \(y\) would die off on its own (predator). We assume that encounters between the species are jointly proportional to the population of each, and they subtract from the prey and add to the predators.

We find fixed points when \(3x-(xy/2)=(xy/4)-y=0\text{,}\) which has two solutions, \((0,0)\) and \((4,6)\text{.}\) The Jacobian matrix of the system is

\begin{equation*} \mathbf{A}(x,y) = \begin{bmatrix} F_x \amp F_y \\ G_x \amp G_y \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 3-y/2 \amp -x/2 \\ y/4 \amp -1+x/4 \end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}

At the origin, the Jacobian becomes

\begin{equation*} \mathbf{A}(0,0) = \begin{bmatrix} 3 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp -1 \end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}

The eigenvalues of a diagonal matrix (in fact, a triangular matrix) are the diagonal entries. Hence the origin is a saddle point.

At the other steady state we have

\begin{equation*} \mathbf{A}(4,6) = \twomat{0}{-2}{3/2}{0}\text{.} \end{equation*}

The characteristic polynomial is \(\lambda^2 - 0\lambda + 3\text{,}\) so the eigenvalues are \(\pm i\sqrt{3}\text{.}\) Hence this fixed point is a center. Close to the point, the orbits are impossible to tell apart from those of a linear center:
