Section 2.10 Steady states

¶

Refer to section 1.7 of the text (pp. 57-58).

We're about to embark on some nonlinear ODEs. First consider a generic first-order problem in the form \(y'=f(y)\text{.}\) Even before attempting a full solution of the problem, we can see that a value \(y_0\) that satisfies \(f(y_0)=0\) is special, because then we get \(y'=0\) and the solution is constant. Such a value is called a steady state or equilibrium.

Consider holding a broom with bristles pointed down using two fingers. This is ideally a pendulum with a steady state of the broom pointing straight down. You will have no difficulty holding the broom in approximately that position as long as you want.

Now imagine trying to hold the broom using the same two fingers, but with the bristles pointing upward. This is an "inverted pendulum," and ideally there is a steady state with the broom pointing straight up. Yet you will find it very difficult to hold the broom in that position for more than a few seconds.

The difference between these steady states is the property of stability. It's impossible to place the broom in exactly the equilibrium position with no motion whatsoever of your hand. In the real world, a steady state is constantly undergoing small perturbations. If the effects of those perturbations die out with time, the steady state is called stable, but if they grow, it is unstable.

Example 2.10.1.

Consider the ODE \(y'=y-y^3\text{.}\) The equilibrium solutions are \(y=0\) and \(y=\pm 1\text{.}\) Consider also that \(y'\lt 0\) if \(-1\lt y \lt 0\) or \(y\gt 1\text{;}\) otherwise, \(y' \gt 0\text{.}\) This allows us to make a phase line diagram clarifying that the steady states at \(y=\pm 1\) are stable, while the one at \(y=0\) is unstable.

The phase line diagram clarifies stability of equilibria. An equivalent analytical test is that \(y' \lt 0\) at a stable equilbrium, and \(y' \gt 0\) at an unstable one.
