Section 4.4 Oscillators

¶

The most common use of second-order equations is the modeling of oscillatory or other periodic behavior.

Subsection 4.4.1 Mechanical

¶

As we have already seen, a mass hanging from a spring satisfies the ODE

\begin{equation*} mx'' + b x' + kx = F(t)\text{.} \end{equation*}

(As is typical, all the named constants are nonnegative.) Here \(m\)> is the mass, \(b\)>is a coefficient of friction or other damping forces, and \(k\)> is a characteristic constant of the spring. The term \(F(t)\) represents an external driving force. If we have initial conditions, they give values for \(x\) and \(x'\text{.}\)

The gravitational force on a pendulum bob (think child on a swing) has a related equation. The proper ODE is

\begin{equation*} \theta'' + \gamma \theta + \frac{g}{L}\sin \theta = F(t)\text{,} \end{equation*}

where \(\theta(t)\) is the angle made with vertical-down and \(L\) is the arm length. This equation is nonlinear. But if the angle remains small, then a reasonable approximation is

\begin{equation*} \theta'' + \gamma \theta + \frac{g}{L}\theta = F(t)\text{,} \end{equation*}

which is a linear oscillator.

Subsection 4.4.2 Electrical oscillators

An AC circuit often has elements of resistance, capacitance, and inductance. These analogize perfectly to friction/damping, spring constant, and mass. If these elements are wired in series with a generator, the governing ODE is

\begin{equation*} LI'' + RI' + CI = \mathcal{E}'(t)\text{,} \end{equation*}

where \(L\) is inductance, \(R\) is resistance, \(C\) is capacitance, and \(I(t)\) is the current flowing through the circuit. The forcing term represents the generator whose voltage (EMF) is \(\mathcal{E}(t)\text{.}\) For alternating current this is modeled as a cosine (or complex exponential) function.

In DC circuits we have Ohm's Law, \(I=V/R\text{,}\) for a resistance \(R\text{.}\) In the AC case, if \(\mathcal{E}(t)=Ve^{i\omega t}\text{,}\) then \(e^{i\omega t}\) appears in the current as well, and it still makes sense to talk about \(V/I\) as a kind of resistance, now called impedance and dependent on the frequency \(\omega\text{.}\)

Subsection 4.4.3 Unifying notation

¶

When you have lots of different versions of the same root problem, with different symbols and units, you have three options. You can solve each new problem from scratch, you can derive custom formulas for each application, or you can try to find a minimal set of parameters and express each problem using those. Let's consider the last pathway here.

The mechanical oscillator \(mx'' + bx' + kx = F(t)\) is more conveniently expressed as

\begin{equation*} x'' + \frac{b}{m} x' + \frac{k}{m} x = \frac{1}{m} F(t)\text{.} \end{equation*}

One reason to prefer this form is dimensional analysis: \(x\) must have the same units as \(F/m\text{,}\) and both \(b/m\) and \(\sqrt{k/m}\) have units of inverse time. We introduce new parameters

\begin{equation*} \omega_0 = \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}} \gt 0, \quad Z = \frac{1}{2} \frac{b/m}{\omega_0} = \frac{b}{\sqrt{4km}}\ge 0\text{.} \end{equation*}

\(\omega_0\) is known as the natural frequency, with units of inverse time, and \(Z\) is a dimensionless number describing the intensity of the damping. Now the ODE becomes

\begin{equation} x'' + 2 Z \omega_0 x' + \omega_0^2 x = \omega_0^2 F(t)\text{.}\label{so-eq-unified}\tag{4.4.1} \end{equation}

A similar derivation can be done starting from the electrical circuit equation.

In math we usually use “frequency” to mean the multiplier of \(t\) in a sin or cos function. In some fields this is called angular frequency and “frequency” is used to mean the number of cycles per time unit, as in Hz.

The eigenvalues of the homogeneous form of this equation are

\begin{equation*} \lambda_{1,2} = -Z \omega_0 \pm \omega_0 \sqrt{Z^2-1}\text{.} \end{equation*}

The discussion now splits into four cases marked by increasing values of \(Z\text{.}\)

Subsection 4.4.4 Undamped

¶

The first case for (4.4.1) is \(Z=0\text{,}\) which is the idealization of no mechanical damping or friction (or resistance, in a circuit). The homogeneous equation is \(x''+\omega_0^2x=0\text{,}\) which has eigenvalues \(\pm i\omega_0\) and general solution

\begin{equation*} x_h(t) = c_1 e^{i\omega_0 t} + c_2 e^{-i\omega_0 t} = a_1 \cos(\omega_0 t) + a_2 \sin(\omega_0 t)\text{.} \end{equation*}

Thus the undamped case results in pure oscillation at frequency \(\omega_0\text{.}\)

Subsection 4.4.5 Underdamped

¶

For \(0\lt Z \lt 1\) the eigenvalues of (4.4.1) are complex:

\begin{equation*} \lambda_{1,2} = -Z \omega_0 \pm i \omega_0 \sqrt{1-Z^2}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Defining \(\omega_d=\omega_0 \sqrt{1-Z^2}\text{,}\) the general homogeneous solution is therefore

\begin{equation*} x_h(t) = e^{-Z\omega_0 t} \left( c_1 e^{i\omega_d t} + c_2 e^{-i\omega_d t} \right)\text{,} \end{equation*}

or the equivalent real form with cos and sin. This solution is pseudoperiodic, combining oscillation at frequency \(\omega_d \lt \omega_0\) inside an exponential decay envelope. This situation is called an underdamped oscillator.

Subsection 4.4.6 Critically damped

¶

At \(Z=1\) the complex eigenvalues collapse to a double eigenvalue at

\begin{equation*} \lambda_1 = \lambda_2 = -\omega_0\text{,} \end{equation*}

with general homogeneous solution

\begin{equation*} x_h(t) = e^{-\omega_0 t} (c_1 + c_2 t)\text{.} \end{equation*}

There is no longer any oscillation present, and we have a critically damped oscillator. The linear growth of \(c_2 t\) doesn't make much difference against the exponential decay.

Subsection 4.4.7 Overdamped

¶

For \(Z>1\) the eigenvalues

\begin{equation*} \lambda_{1,2} = -Z \omega_0 \pm \omega_0 \sqrt{Z^2-1} \end{equation*}

are negative and real, thus giving an exponentially decaying homogeneous solution. In this case we have an overdamped oscillator.
