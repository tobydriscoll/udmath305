Section 4.5 Driven oscillator

¶

We next want to examine (4.4.1) when the forcing function is an exponential:

\begin{equation} x'' + 2 Z \omega_0 x' + \omega_0^2 x = \omega_0^2 e^{rt}\text{.}\label{so-eq-unifiedexp}\tag{4.5.1} \end{equation}

We're interested in the undamped and underdamped cases, \(0\le Z \lt 1\text{.}\)

It's possible to use variation of parameters as in Section 3.7 to derive a particular solution, but it's overkill here. Instead we just appeal to the method of undetermined coefficients, positing a solution in the form \(x_p(t) = A e^{rt}\) to derive

\begin{equation*} (r^2 + 2 Z \omega_0 r + \omega_0^2)Ae^{rt} = \omega_0^2 e^{rt}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Hence

\begin{equation} A(r) = \frac{\omega_0^2}{r^2 + 2 Z \omega_0 r + \omega_0^2}\label{so-eq-coeff}\tag{4.5.2} \end{equation}

gives a particular solution unless the denominator is zero, i.e., \(r\) is an eigenvalue (since the denominator is the characteristic polynomial). More on that situation later.

Subsection 4.5.1 Harmonic forcing

¶

Exponential forcing \(F(t)=e^{rt}\) is particulary important when \(r\) is imaginary. Say \(r=i\omega\text{.}\) Then by Euler's identity we have

\begin{equation*} F(t) = \cos(\omega t) + i\sin(\omega t)\text{.} \end{equation*}

Thanks to linearity, if given a cos or sin forcing, we can solve the complex-valued problem and then take a real or imaginary part. That is, if \(x_p(t) = A e^{i\omega t}\) as above, then

\begin{align*} [\Re x_p]'' + 2 Z \omega_0 [\Re x_p]' + \omega_0^2 [\Re x_p] \amp = \omega_0^2 \cos(\omega t), \\ [\Im x_p]'' + 2 Z \omega_0 [\Im x_p]' + \omega_0^2 [\Im x_p] \amp = \omega_0^2 \sin(\omega t). \end{align*}

It's often useful to rewrite the complex factor \(A(i\omega)\) from (4.5.2) in polar form:

\begin{equation*} A(i\omega) = \frac{\omega_0^2}{\omega_0^2-\omega^2 + 2 Z i \omega_0 \omega } = g(\omega) e^{-i\phi(\omega)}\text{,} \end{equation*}

where \(g=|A|\) is the gain and \(\phi\) is the phase lag. Note that the particular solution is

\begin{equation*} x_p(t) = g(\omega) e^{-i\phi(\omega)} e^{i\omega t} = g e^{i(\omega t-\phi)}\text{.} \end{equation*}

This means that as we pass from the input forcing to the output solution, \(g\) is the scaling of the amplitude and \(\phi\) changes the phase of our trip around the complex unit circle.

Subsection 4.5.2 Resonance

¶

Consider forcing \(e^{i\omega t}\) in the undamped case with \(Z=0\text{.}\) Then

\begin{equation*} A(i\omega) = \frac{\omega_0^2}{\omega_0^2-\omega^2} \end{equation*}

is purely real, and the general solution is

Fact 4.5.1. Solution for undamped driven oscillator (not resonant).
\begin{equation*} x(t) = x_h(t) + x_p(t) = c_1 e^{i\omega_0 t} + c_2 e^{-i\omega_0 t} + \frac{\omega_0^2}{\omega_0^2-\omega^2} e^{i\omega t} \end{equation*}

(or a real equivalent), provided \(\omega\neq\omega_0\)

If \(\omega\approx \omega_0\text{,}\) then the amplitude of the particular solution is very large.

What is the meaning if \(\omega = \omega_0\text{?}\) This is exactly when \(i\omega\) is an eigenvalue of the homogeneous problem, so the method of undetermined coefficients will actually fail like in Example 4.3.4. But there is a sneaky shortcut. For a certain choice of \(c_1\) and \(c_2\text{,}\) we get a different particular solution

\begin{equation*} \hat{x}_p(t) = \frac{\omega_0^2}{\omega_0^2-\omega^2} \left( e^{i\omega t} - e^{i\omega_0 t} \right)\text{,} \end{equation*}

which has an indeterminate form in the limit \(\omega \to \omega_0\text{,}\) allowing us to use L'Hopital's Rule:

\begin{equation*} \lim_{\omega \to \omega_0} \hat{x}_p(t) = \lim_{\omega \to \omega_0} \omega_0^2 \frac{it e^{i\omega t}}{-2\omega} = -\frac{i\omega_0^2}{2} t e^{i\omega_0 t}\text{.} \end{equation*}

(The derivation is subtle because the derivatives here are with respect to the limited variable \(\omega\) and not \(t\text{.}\)) This leads to the general solution

Fact 4.5.2. Solution for undamped driven oscillator at resonance.
\begin{equation*} x(t) = c_1 e^{i\omega_0 t} + c_2 e^{-i\omega_0 t} -\frac{i\omega_0^2}{2} t e^{i\omega_0 t} \end{equation*}

(or a real equivalent)

This particular solution oscillates at the natural/driving frequency but with a linearly growing amplitude. This situation is called resonance and is one of the most important phenomena in physics.

The eternally growing amplitude in resonance is a byproduct of the idealized undamped model. What happens if \(Z\gt 0\text{?}\) We are back to \(x_p=Ae^{i\omega t}\text{,}\) with

\begin{equation*} A(i\omega) = \frac{\omega_0^2}{\omega_0^2-\omega^2 + 2 Z i \omega_0\,\omega }\text{.} \end{equation*}

Of particular interest is the gain,

Fact 4.5.3. Gain for damped driven oscillator.
\begin{equation*} g(\omega) = |A(i\omega)| = \frac{\omega_0^2}{[(\omega^2-\omega_0^2)^2 + 4 Z^2 \omega_0^2\,\omega^2]^{1/2} } \end{equation*}

This formula is a bit easier to parse in terms of the frequency ratio \(\rho=\omega/\omega_0\text{:}\)

\begin{equation*} g(\rho) = \frac{1}{[(1-\rho^2)^2 + 4 Z^2 \rho^2]^{1/2} }\text{.} \end{equation*}

Basic calculus shows that the denominator is minimized, and thus the gain is maximized, when

\begin{equation*} \rho_\text{max} = \sqrt{1-2Z^2}, \quad \text{ if } Z^2 \le \frac{1}{2}\text{,} \end{equation*}

and \(\rho=0\) if \(\frac{1}{2} \lt Z^2 \lt 1\text{.}\) Since \(\rho_\text{max} \lt 1\text{,}\) the “most resonant” frequency is lower than the natural frequency. For \(Z^2 \le \frac{1}{2}\) the maximum gain is

\begin{equation*} g(\rho_\text{max}) = \frac{1}{2Z\sqrt{1-Z^2}}\text{,} \end{equation*}

which is finite but large for \(Z\approx 0\text{.}\)
