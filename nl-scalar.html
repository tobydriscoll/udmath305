Section 5.2 Scalar first-order problems

¶

Scalar, first-order nonlinear problems are often more penetrable than more general equations. If nothing else, the problem

\begin{equation*} x' = f(t,x) \end{equation*}

is easily visualized. By evaluating \(f\) on a grid in the \((t,x)\) plane, we can draw arrows representing the slope \(dx/dt\) of the solution curves that pass through the grid points. This picture is known as a direction field.

Example 5.2.1. Direction field for a scalar problem.

Here is a direction field for \(x'=t-x^2\text{.}\) Note that the arrows are horizontal along the sideways parabola \(t=x^2\text{.}\) Curves with this property are known as nullclines.

Subsection 5.2.1 Autonomous equations

¶

Problems of the form \(x'=f(x)\) are called autonomous. In autonomous problems, a root of the function \(f\) is an equilibrium or steady-state solution. As we saw in linear systems, the stability of equilibrium solutions is often important.

The direction field of an autonomous equation is independent of \(t\text{.}\) A different picture known as a phase line diagram can help to discriminate between stable and unstable equilibria. This is most readily explained with an example.

Example 5.2.3.

Consider the ODE \(x'=x-x^3\text{.}\) The equilibrium solutions are \(x=0\) and \(x=\pm 1\text{,}\) which is clear in a plot of \(f(x)\text{.}\) The plot also shows the sign of \(f(x)\) and therefore of \(dx/dt\text{.}\) So between the equilibria we can draw arrows to show whether \(x\) is increasing or decreasing with time. The arrows clarify that the steady states at \(x=\pm 1\) are stable, while the one at \(x=0\) is unstable.

A simple analytical test for the stability of an equilibrium value \(x_e\) is that \(f'(x_e) \lt 0\) at a stable equilibrium, and \(f'(x_e) \gt 0\) at an unstable one. This too is clear from the phase-line diagram. The case \(f'(x_e)=0\) requires additional investigation.

Subsection 5.2.2 Separable equations

¶

All problems in the autonomous form \(x'=f(x)\text{,}\) and more generally in the form \(x'=f(x)g(t)\text{,}\) are called separable equations and can be solved systematically (up to performing integrations). Rather than deriving a formula for them, it's best to just repeat a straightforward process for each new problem.

Example 5.2.5.

Consider the variable growth archetype 2.1.6 \(x'=2tx\text{.}\) We express \(x'\) as \(dx/dt\) and then isolate the variables:

\begin{equation*} \frac{dx}{x} = 2t\,dt\text{.} \end{equation*}

Integrating both sides leads to \(\log |x| = t^2 + C\text{,}\) or \(|x|=Ae^{t^2}\) for a positive constant \(A\) (since it is the exponential of a real constant). Taking the absolute value off of \(x\) means that \(A\) can be negative as well. Also, \(A=0\) clearly leads to a solution. Finally, we conclude that \(x=Ce^{t^2}\) for arbitrary \(C\text{.}\)

Example 5.2.6.

Suppose \(x'=t^2/(x^3-2)\text{.}\) Separation and integration lead to

\begin{equation*} \int (x^3-2) \, dx = \int t^2\, dt\text{,} \end{equation*}

or \(\frac{1}{4}x^4 - 2x = C + \frac{1}{3}t^3\text{.}\) We could work hard to try to solve explicitly for \(y\text{,}\) but it's probably best to leave it in implicit form. This is a common limitation to separable "solutions".

Even in implicit form, we can solve for the arbitrary constant if given an initial condition. For instance, suppose \(x(0)=4\) is given for this ODE. Then \(4^4/4-2\cdot4 = C+0\text{,}\) so \(C=56\text{.}\)

Sometimes the separable structure isn't immediately apparent, and you have to manipulate the expressions a bit.

Example 5.2.7.

Suppose \(t x' = x-t x\text{.}\) This does not look separable until you see that you can factor out \(x\) on the right side. Then we have \(dx/dt = x(1-t)/t\text{,}\) or

\begin{equation*} \frac{dx}{x} = (t^{-1}-1)\,dt\text{.} \end{equation*}

Thus \(\ln|x| = \ln|t|-t+C\text{,}\) or \(x=A t e^{-t}\text{.}\)

Note that this problem is also linear, so it could be approached that way as well. Of course you must get the same solution in the end!

Exercises 5.2.3 Exercises

¶
1. Change of variable.

The equation

\begin{equation*} x' = x + t x^2 \end{equation*}

is neither linear nor separable. However, show that the change of variable \(u=x^{-1}\) transforms it into

\begin{equation*} u' + u = -t\text{,} \end{equation*}

which is linear. Solve this equation and then use that to solve the original ODE.

Answer

\(x = \dfrac{1}{c_1 e^{-t} + 1 - t}\)

2. Flames in space.

A simple model for the radius of a ball of flame in zero gravity is \(r'=r^2-r^3\text{,}\) because oxygen intake is proportional to surface area while fuel consumption is proportional to volume. Find an implicit solution of this equation.

Answer
\begin{equation*} \log\left(\frac{r}{1-r}\right) - \frac{1}{r} = t + c_1 \end{equation*}
