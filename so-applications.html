Section 2.6 Applications of oscillators

¶

From 2.5 in the text, but very little from the electrical side. Also go back to "better notation" from 2.4.

Oscillations appear all over science and engineering, and 2nd order linear equations are often used to model them. The best-known examples are mechanical and electrical, but there are chemical and biochemical ones as well.

Subsection 2.6.1 Mechanical oscillators

As we have already seen, a mass hanging from a spring satisfies the ODE

\begin{equation*} my'' + b y' + ky = f(t)\text{.} \end{equation*}

(As is typical, all the named constants are nonnegative.) Here \(m\)> is the mass, \(b\)>is a coefficient of friction or other damping forces, and \(k\)> is a characteristic constant of the spring. The term \(f(t)\) represents an external driving force. If we have initial conditions, they give values for \(y\) and \(y'\text{.}\)

The gravitational force on a pendulum bob (think child on a swing) has a related equation. The proper ODE is

\begin{equation*} \theta'' + \gamma \theta + \frac{g}{L}\sin \theta = f(t)\text{,} \end{equation*}

where \(\theta(t)\) is the angle made with vertical-down and \(L\) is the arm length. This equation is nonlinear. But if the angle remains small, then a reasonable approximation is

\begin{equation*} \theta'' + \gamma \theta + \frac{g}{L}\theta = f(t)\text{,} \end{equation*}

which is a linear oscillator.

Subsection 2.6.2 Electrical oscillators

An AC circuit often has elements of resistance, capacitance, and inductance. These analogize perfectly to friction/damping, spring constant, and mass. If these elements are wired in series with a generator, the governing ODE is

\begin{equation*} LI'' + RI' + CI = \mathcal{E}'(t)\text{,} \end{equation*}

where \(L\) is inductance, \(R\) is resistance, \(C\) is capacitance, and \(I(t)\) is the current flowing through the circuit. The forcing term represents the generator whose voltage (EMF) is \(\mathcal{E}(t)\text{.}\) For alternating current this is modeled as a cosine (or complex exponential) function.

In DC circuits we have Ohm's Law, \(I=V/R\text{,}\) for a resistance \(R\text{.}\) In the AC case, if \(\mathcal{E}(t)=Ve^{i\omega t}\text{,}\) then \(e^{i\omega t}\) appears in the current as well, and it still makes sense to talk about \(V/I\) as a kind of resistance, now called impedance, written as \(Z\text{,}\) and dependent on the frequency \(\omega\text{.}\) The impedance is very closely related to the transfer function for \(s=i\omega\text{.}\)

Subsection 2.6.3 Unifying notation

¶

When you have lots of different versions of the same root problem, with different symbols and units, you have three options. You can solve each new problem from scratch, you can derive custom formulas for each application, or you can try to find a minimal set of parameters and express each problem using those. Let's consider the last pathway here.

The mechanical oscillator \(Ay'' + By' + Cy = f(t)\) is more conveniently expressed as

\begin{equation*} y'' + \frac{B}{A} y' + \frac{C}{A} y + \frac{C}{A} F(t)\text{.} \end{equation*}

One reason to prefer this form is that \(y\) must have the same units as the forcing \(F\text{.}\) Furthermore, we see the undamped natural frequency \(\omega_n=\sqrt{C/A}\text{,}\) and this has units of inverse time only. The same units apply to \(B/A\text{,}\) so we introduce the new dimensionless parameter

\begin{equation*} Z = \frac{1}{2} \frac{B/A}{\omega_n} = \frac{B}{\sqrt{4AC}}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Now the ODE becomes

\begin{equation*} y'' + 2 Z \omega_n y' + \omega_n^2 y = \omega_n^2 F(t)\text{.} \end{equation*}

The characteristic roots are now a bit more transparent: \(-Z \omega_n \pm \omega_n \sqrt{Z^2-1}\text{.}\) We see that the condition for being underdamped is simply \(Z \lt 1\text{.}\) In this case we express the roots as \(-Z \omega_n \pm i \omega_d\text{,}\) using the damped frequency \(\omega_d=\omega_n\sqrt{1-Z^2}\text{.}\)

Continuing the underdamped case, the gain works out to be

\begin{equation*} G^2 = \frac{1}{(\omega_n^2-\omega^2)^2 + 4\omega_n^2Z^2\omega^2} = \frac{\omega_n^{-4}}{(1-\rho^2)^2 + 4Z^2\rho^2}\text{,} \end{equation*}

where \(\rho=\omega/\omega_n\) is a dimensionless frequency ratio. If also \(Z^2\lt 1/2\text{,}\) the gain is maximized when \(\rho=\sqrt{1-2Z^2} \lt 1\text{.}\) The gain at that maximum is proportional to \(1/Z\text{,}\) so much growth in the response is possible near the natural frequency when damping is small in the sense of \(Z\approx 0\text{.}\) This can be considered a practical resonance for lightly damped systems.

Example 2.6.1

The oscillator \(4y'' + y' + 36y\) from an earlier example has \(\omega_n=3\) and \(Z=1/\sqrt{16\cdot 36}=1/24\text{.}\) Accordingly, gain is maximized when \(\omega/\omega_n=1-\frac{1}{288}\approx 0.9965\text{,}\) so for practical purposes a resonance occurs at just slightly less than the natural, undamped frequency.
