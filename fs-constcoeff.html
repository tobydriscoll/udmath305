Section 3.3 Constant-coefficient systems

¶

The solutions of \(\bx'=\bA(t)\bx\) largely boil down to finding a fundamental matrix, which satisfies

\begin{equation*} \bX' = \bigl[ \bx_1' \: \bx_2' \: \cdots \: \bx_n' \bigr] = \bigl[ \bA\bx_1 \: \bA\bx_2 \: \cdots \: \bA\bx_n \bigr] = \bA \bX\text{.} \end{equation*}

With such a matrix in hand, the general solution is \(\bx(t)=\bX(t)\bc\) for an arbitrary “integration constant” \(\bc\text{.}\)

The good news for the general case pretty much ends there; in most problems we cannot get a reasonably simple expression for \(\bX\text{.}\) But there is a very important special case in which we can say a lot: when \(\bA(t)\) is a constant matrix.

We begin the journey with an eigenpair of \(\bA\text{,}\)

\begin{equation*} \bA \bv = \lambda \bv\text{.} \end{equation*}

If we posit that \(\bx(t)=g(t)\bv\text{,}\) then

\begin{equation*} g'\bv = \bA(g\bv) = g(\bA\bv) = g\lambda \bv\text{,} \end{equation*}

which can be satisfied if \(g'=\lambda g\text{,}\) or \(g(t)=ce^{\lambda t}\text{.}\) That is, each eigenpair gives us a homogeneous solution! (Yes, this the moment all the eigenstuff was preparing us for.)

Counting algebraic multiplicities, we know that \(\bA\) has eigenvalues \(\lambda_1,\ldots,\lambda_n\text{.}\) Say that we have eigenvectors \(\bv_1,\ldots,\bv_n\) to go with them. Then we have \(n\) homogeneous solutions

\begin{equation*} \bx_j(t) = e^{\lambda_j t} \bv_j, \quad j=1,\ldots,n\text{.} \end{equation*}

To determine whether they form a fundamental matrix, we need to determine whether they form a basis. According to Theorem 3.2.3, we can ask that question at any value of \(t\text{,}\) including \(t=0\text{.}\) So the key issue is the independence of the eigenvectors.

Theorem 3.3.1. Solution by eigenvectors.

Let \(\bA\) have eigenvalues \(\lambda_1,\ldots,\lambda_n\) and corresponding eigenvectors \(\bv_1,\ldots,\bv_n\text{.}\) If the eigenvectors are independent (that is, they form a basis), then

\begin{equation} \bX(t) = \begin{bmatrix} e^{\lambda_1 t} \bv_1 \amp \cdots \amp e^{\lambda_n t} \bv_n \end{bmatrix}\label{fs-eq-eigensolution}\tag{3.3.1} \end{equation}

is a fundamental matrix for \(\bx'=\bA\bx\text{.}\)

This situation occurs when \(\bA\) has no defective eigenvalues, i.e., it is a diagonalizable matrix (Section 1.12). One important case when that is guaranteed is when the eigenvalues are all distinct (Theorem 1.11.16).

Example 3.3.2.

We can find the eigenstuff of

\begin{equation*} \bA = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 1 \\ 4 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix} \end{equation*}

as \(\lambda_1=3\text{,}\) \(\bv_1 = \twovec{1}{2}\text{,}\) and \(\lambda_2=-1\text{,}\) \(\bv_2=\twovec{1}{-2}\text{.}\) This gives us solutions of \(\bx'=\bA\bx\text{:}\)

\begin{equation*} \bx_1(t) = e^{3t} \twovec{1}{2},\quad \bx_2(t) = e^{-t} \twovec{1}{-2} \text{.} \end{equation*}

Because the eigenvalues are distinct, we know that these are independent solutions. The general homogeneous solution is

\begin{equation*} \bx(t) = \bX(t)\bc = c_1 e^{3t} \twovec{1}{2} + c_2 e^{-t} \twovec{1}{-2} = \twovec{ c_1 e^{3t} + c_2 e^{-t}}{2c_1 e^{3t} - 2 c_2e^{-t}}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Matrices with real, distinct eigenvalues will always look like that example. Things get trickier with complex eigenvalues and defective eigenvalues, and we need to spend some time with each.
