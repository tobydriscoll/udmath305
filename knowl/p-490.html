This formula is a bit easier to parse in terms of the frequency ratio \(\rho=\omega/\omega_0\text{:}\)

\begin{equation*} g(\rho) = \frac{1}{\omega_0^2 [(1-\rho^2)^2 + 4 Z^2 \rho^2]^{1/2} }\text{.} \end{equation*}

In the presence of any damping, the gain always remains finite, and there is no longer a perfect resonance. But as a function of the frequency ratio, the denominator is minimized, and thus the gain is maximized, when

\begin{equation} \rho_\text{max} = \begin{cases} \sqrt{1-2Z^2}, \amp \text{ if } 0 \lt Z^2 \le \frac{1}{2},\\ 0, \amp \text{ if } \frac{1}{2} \lt Z^2 \lt 1. \end{cases}\text{,}\tag{4.5.3} \end{equation}

Since \(\rho_\text{max} \lt 1\text{,}\) the “most resonant” frequency is always lower than the natural frequency. For \(Z^2 \le \frac{1}{2}\) the maximum gain is

\begin{equation} g(\rho_\text{max}) = \frac{1}{2Z\omega_0^2\sqrt{1-Z^2}}\text{,}\tag{4.5.4} \end{equation}

which is finite but large for \(Z\approx 0\text{.}\)
