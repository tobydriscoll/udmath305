The mechanical oscillator \(mx'' + bx' + kx = F(t)\) has, like all linear ODEs, a homogeneous part and a forced part. For now we consider only the homogeneous part, taking up the forcing term in the next section. Ignoring the degenerate case of zero mass, we write

\begin{equation*} x'' + \frac{b}{m} x' + \frac{k}{m} x = 0\text{.} \end{equation*}

One reason to prefer this form is dimensional analysis: both \(b/m\) and \(\sqrt{k/m}\) have units of inverse time. We introduce new parameters

\begin{equation*} \omega_0 = \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}} \gt 0, \quad Z = \frac{b/m}{2\omega_0} = \frac{b}{\sqrt{4km}} \ge 0\text{.} \end{equation*}

\(\omega_0\) is known as the natural frequency, with units of inverse time, and \(Z\) is a dimensionless damping coefficient describing the relative intensity of the damping. Now the unforced ODE becomes

\begin{equation} x'' + 2 Z \omega_0\, x' + \omega_0^2\, x = 0\text{.}\tag{4.4.1} \end{equation}

A similar derivation can be done starting from the electrical circuit equation.
