In vector calc you learned about the dot product between two vectors. This generalizes into \(n\) dimensions. Suppose \(\bu\) and \(\bv\) are real column vectors of length \(n\text{.}\) Then their dot product, or what we will call the inner product, is

\begin{equation*} \langle \bu, \bv \rangle = \bu \cdot \bv = \overline{u_1} v_1 + \overline{u_2} v_2 + \cdots \overline{u_n} v_n = \sum_k \overline{u_k} v_k\text{.} \end{equation*}

Notice that we introduced complex conjugates onto the \(u_k\) terms. One motivation is that it makes the inner product of a vector with itself guaranteed to be a nonnegative real number:

\begin{equation*} \langle \bu, \bu \rangle = \sum_k \overline{u_k} u_k = \sum_k |u_k|^2 \ge 0\text{.} \end{equation*}

In fact we define a function called a norm by \(\| \bu \| = \sqrt{ \langle \bu, \bu \rangle}\text{.}\) This generalizes familiar Euclidean distance into \(n\) dimensions and complex-valued vectors.
