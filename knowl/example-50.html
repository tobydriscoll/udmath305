Example 4.2.2 Singular system of equations

Elimination for the singular system

\begin{align*} \amp x \amp+ 2 y = \amp 1\\ \amp {-2} x \amp- 4 y = \amp {-1} \end{align*}

leads in one step to

\begin{align*} \amp x \amp+ 2 y = \amp 1\\ \amp 0 x \amp + 0 y = \amp 1\text{.} \end{align*}

The second equation is impossible to satisfy, hence there are no solutions. On the other hand, if we started with

\begin{align*} \amp x \amp+ 2 y = \amp {-3}\\ \amp {-2} x \amp- 4 y = \amp 6 \end{align*}

then we get

\begin{align*} \amp x \amp+ 2 y = \amp {-3}\\ \amp 0 x \amp + 0 y = \amp 0\text{.} \end{align*}

The second equation is satisfied no matter what we do. So we are "free" to choose \(y\text{,}\) and then the first equation tells us that \(x=-3-2y\text{.}\) In vector form, the system is solved by

\begin{equation*} \twovec{-3}{0} + y \twovec{-2}{1} \end{equation*}

for any choice of \(y\text{.}\) We may interpret this as "particular plus null," because \(\bA \twovec{-2}{1}=\boldsymbol{0}\text{.}\)
