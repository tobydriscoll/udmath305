Example 3.4.3.

Here are some looks at the function \(e^{\lambda t}\) for different complex values of \(\lambda\text{.}\) First, when \(\lambda\) is purely imaginary, the function values stay on the unit circle in the complex plane. Taking the real and imaginary parts of the function give cosine and sine, respectively.

If \(\Re(\lambda) \gt 0\text{,}\) the magnitude of the function grows exponentially. The result is an outward spiral. The imaginary part of \(\lambda\) controls the frequency or "tightness" of the spiral.

Finally, if \(\Re(\lambda) \lt 0\text{,}\) the spiral is a decaying one. The real and imaginary parts are attenuated oscillations.
