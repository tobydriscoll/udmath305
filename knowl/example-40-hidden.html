Suppose a mug of coffee at 90 C is put in a room kept at 20 C. In terms of the notation above, we have

\begin{equation*} \frac{dx}{dt} = a (x-20), \quad x(0)=90\text{.} \end{equation*}

We could also pose it for the temperature difference \(z=x-20\) as \(z'=az\text{.}\) Either way it's a linear problem with solution

\begin{equation*} x(t) = Ce^{at} + 20\text{.} \end{equation*}

We determine \(C\) from the initial value: \(C=70\text{.}\) To get a value for \(a\text{,}\) we need the temperature at another time too. Say the coffee has cooled by \(7\) degrees in 10 minutes. Then \(83 = 70e^{10a}+20\text{,}\) or \(a=0.1\ln(0.9) \approx -0.01054\) per min. The coffee will reach 60 C when \(40 = 70e^{at}\text{,}\) or \(t\approx 53.1\) minutes.
