Example 3.5.1.

We eariler found the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of

\begin{equation*} \bA = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp -1 \\ 5 \amp -3 \end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}

The result is

\begin{equation*} \lambda_{1,2} = -1 \pm 1i, \qquad \bv_{1,2} = \twovec{1}{2\mp i}\text{.} \end{equation*}

This gives a general solution in the form

\begin{equation*} \bx(t) = c_1 e^{(-1+i)t} \twovec{1}{2- i} + c_2 e^{(-1-i)t} \twovec{1}{2+i}\text{.} \end{equation*}

It's implied that \(c_{1,2}\) are complex constants.

There is more to the story. A purely real problem, with real initial conditions, will lead to a solution that is real at all times. In that case it works out that \(c_2=\overline{c_1}\text{.}\) But an alternative way to express the solution is to take the real and imaginary parts of

\begin{align*} e^{(-1+i)t} \twovec{1}{2-i} \amp = e^{-t} [cos(t)+i\sin(t)] \left( \twovec{1}{2} + i\twovec{0}{-1} \right)\\ \amp = e^{-t} \left( cos(t)\twovec{1}{2} - \sin(t) \twovec{0}{-1} \right) + i e^{-t} \left( cos(t)\twovec{0}{-1} + \sin(t) \twovec{1}{2} \right) \text{.} \end{align*}

That is, the general solution can also be written as

\begin{equation*} \bx(t) = b_1 e^{-t} \twovec{\cos(t)}{2\cos(t)+\sin(t)} + b_2 e^{-t}\twovec{\sin(t)}{2\sin(t)-\cos(t)} \text{,} \end{equation*}

where here \(b_1\) and \(b_2\) are real.
