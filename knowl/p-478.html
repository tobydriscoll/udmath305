We appeal to the method of undetermined coefficients, positing a solution in the form \(x_p(t) = A e^{rt}\) to derive

\begin{equation*} (r^2 + 2 Z \omega_0 r + \omega_0^2)Ae^{rt} = e^{rt}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Hence

\begin{equation} x_p(t) = A e^{rt}, \qquad A(r) = \frac{1}{r^2 + 2 Z \omega_0 r + \omega_0^2}\tag{4.5.2} \end{equation}

gives a particular solution unless the denominator, which is really just the characteristic polynomial, is zero. In that case, \(r\) is an eigenvalue; we consider that situation later.
