In light of the last example, let's look again at the general case

\begin{equation*} X(s) = \frac{ (s+b)x(0)+x'(0)}{s^2+bs+c} + \frac{F(s)}{s^2 +bs+c}\text{.} \end{equation*}

The denominators contain the characteristic polynomial, whose roots are the eigenvalues of the homogeneous problem. We refer to zeros in the denominator as poles of the transform. Each eigenvalue pole will contribute a term \(1/(s-\lambda)\) to the partial fraction decomposition. Upon inversion, this term contributes \(e^{\lambda t}\text{,}\) which is part of the homogeneous solution. The forcing function will contribute one or more of its own poles in \(F(s)\text{,}\) and they generate new terms in the partial fraction decomposition.
