This is our first partial differential equation. There are two independent variables now, \(x\) and \(t\text{.}\) Like our earlier ODEs in time, it allows an initial condition,

\begin{equation*} \rho(0,x) = f(x)\text{,} \end{equation*}

and like our ODEs in space, two boundary conditions,

\begin{equation*} \rho(t,0) = \rho(t,\pi) = 0\text{.} \end{equation*}

(Aren't all subway cars exactly \(\pi\) long?) Conditions like these on the value of the dependent variable at the boundary are called Dirichlet conditions. Conditions that are instead on the first spatial derivative,

\begin{equation*} \pp{\rho}{x}(t,0) = \pp{\rho}{x}(t,\pi) = 0\text{,} \end{equation*}

are called Neumann conditions. They are restrictions on the flux (which is much more realistic for our original subway car example).
