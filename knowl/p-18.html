Suppose the ODE solution is expressed in terms of some infinite power series with unknown coefficients:

\begin{equation*} y(t) = c_0 + c_1 t + c_2 t^2 + c_3 t^3 + c_4 t^4 + \cdots\text{.} \end{equation*}

We can easily differentiate term by term to get

\begin{equation*} y'(t) = c_1 + 2c_2 t + 3c_3 t^2 + 4c_4 t^3 + \cdots\text{.} \end{equation*}

Setting each coefficient here equal to its match in the series for \(ay(t)\) leads to the equations

\begin{equation*} c_1 = ac_0, \quad c_2=a c_1/2, \quad c_3=ac_2/3, \quad \ldots\text{,} \end{equation*}

from which we quickly derive

\begin{equation*} y(t) = c_0\left( 1 + a t + \frac{1}{2} a^2 t^2 + \frac{1}{6} a^3 t^3 + \frac{1}{4!} a^4 t^4 + \cdots \right) = c_0 f(t)\text{.} \end{equation*}

The undetermined constant \(c_0\) plays the role of the integration constant. In fact, since \(f(0)=1\text{,}\) we see that \(c_0=y(0)\text{,}\) so it's convenient to write the solution as \(y(t)=y(0)f(t)\text{,}\) with the understanding that \(y(0)\) is arbitrary.
