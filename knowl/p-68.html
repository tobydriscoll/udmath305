Time for some payoff. If we force the ODE \(y'=ay+q(t)\) using \(q(t)=Re^{i\omega t}\text{,}\) we can take the real part to get the result of cosine forcing, or the imaginary part to get of a sine forcing. Moreover, finding a particular solution with exponential forcing remains very easy. To make \(y=Ye^{i\omega t}\) a particular solution, we need only require that

\begin{equation*} i\omega Y e^{i\omega t} = a Ye^{i\omega t} + Re^{i\omega t} \quad \Rightarrow \quad Y = R \cdot \frac{1}{i\omega - a}\text{.} \end{equation*}

That is, the particular solution is just \(\frac{1}{i\omega - a}\) times the input signal. Let's express this multiplicative factor in polar form:

\begin{equation*} \frac{1}{i\omega - a} = G e^{-i\alpha}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Then the relationship between the forcing signal \(q(t)\) and the particular solution is

\begin{equation*} y(t) = G e^{-i\alpha} q(t) = (GR) e^{i(\omega t - \alpha)}\text{.} \end{equation*}
