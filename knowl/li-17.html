Item 3
Resonant term. We know that terms such as \(te^{zt}\) appear due to repeated roots and resonances. The clever way to get its transform is to think about differentiation with respect to \(z\):
\begin{equation*} \mathcal{L} \left[ \frac{d}{dz} e^{zt} \right] = \frac{d}{dz} \mathcal{L} \left[ e^{zt} \right] = \frac{d}{dz}\left[ \frac{1}{s-z} \right] \text{,} \end{equation*}
from which we conclude
\begin{equation*} \mathcal{L} \left[ t e^{zt} \right] = \frac{1}{(s-z)^2} \text{.} \end{equation*}
If we differentiate again we find
\begin{equation*} \mathcal{L} \left[ t^2 e^{zt} \right] = \frac{2}{(s-z)^3}\text{,} \end{equation*}
and in general
\begin{equation*} \mathcal{L} \left[ t^n e^{zt} \right] = \frac{n!}{(s-z)^{n+1}} \text{.} \end{equation*}
As a special case, taking \(z=0\) allows us to transform any power of \(t\text{,}\) and therefore any polynomial.
