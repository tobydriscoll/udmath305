Exponential forcing is particularly important when \(r\) is imaginary. Set \(r=i\omega\) in the above. The particular solution

\begin{equation*} x_p(t) = A(i\omega) e^{i\omega t} \end{equation*}

is complex; say we decompose it as \(x_p(t)=u_p(t)+i\, v_p(t)\text{.}\) Using operator notation to write \(\opA[x_p]=e^{i\omega t}\text{,}\) linearity implies

\begin{equation*} \opA[u_p] + i\,\opA[v_p] = \cos(\omega t) + i\sin(\omega t)\text{.} \end{equation*}

Matching real and imaginary parts gives us

\begin{align*} \opA[u_p] \amp = \cos(\omega t), \\ \opA[v_p] \amp = \sin(\omega t). \end{align*}

In words, the particular solution for imaginary-exponential forcing gives us the solutions we need for all cosine and sine forcing. All combinations of such forcing functions can be described as harmonic forcing.
