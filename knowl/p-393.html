Of course this result requires us to say what a determinant is beyond the 2-by-2 case. One way to compute a determinant is as the product of pivots in the elimination process, provided you flip the sign for each row swap. This makes the connection to singularity clear, and it's the best way for a computer to find the determinant, but most humans prefer a recursive method called cofactor expansion:

\begin{equation*} \det(\bA) = \sum (-1)^{i+j} A_{ij} \det( \mathbf{M}_{ij} )\text{,} \end{equation*}

where the sum is taken over any row or column of \(\bA\text{,}\) and \(\mathbf{M}_{ij}\) is the matrix that results from deleting row \(i\) and column \(j\) from \(\bA\text{.}\) (You may have learned vector cross products as a 3-by-3 determinant using these cofactors across the top row.)
