Let's consider the \(2\times 2\) case for simplicity. The general solution is

\begin{equation*} \bx(t) = \bX(t)\bc = c_1 e^{\lambda_1 t} \bv_1 + c_2 e^{\lambda_2 t} \bv_2\text{.} \end{equation*}

If \(\bA\) is real, then \(\lambda_2=\overline{\lambda_1}\) and \(\bv_2=\overline{\bv_1}\text{.}\) If the initial conditions are also real, then it works out that \(c_2=\overline{c_1}\) as well, and the entire solution can be expressed by expanding each item into real and imaginary parts. This is, as we say in the biz, not fun.
