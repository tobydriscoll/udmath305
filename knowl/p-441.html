To find the eigenvectors we look for the nullspaces of \(\bA-\lambda_j\bI\) for \(j=1,2\text{.}\) These have the basis vectors \([1,\,\lambda_j]\text{,}\) if the eigenvalues are distinct. Thus the general homogeneous solution is

\begin{equation*} \bu_h(t) = c_1 e^{\lambda_1 t} \twovec{1}{\lambda_1} + c_2 e^{\lambda_2 t} \twovec{1}{\lambda_2}\text{.} \end{equation*}

In terms of the original variable \(x=u_1\text{,}\) this is simply

\begin{equation} x_h(t) = c_1 e^{\lambda_1 t} + c_2 e^{\lambda_2 t}, \qquad \text{if } \lambda_1\neq\lambda_2\text{.}\tag{4.2.1} \end{equation}

If the eigenvalues are complex and written as \(\alpha \pm i \beta\text{,}\) we might want an alternative real-only form. Starting with

\begin{equation*} e^{(\alpha + i\beta)t} = e^{\alpha t} [\cos(\beta t) + i \sin(\beta t)], \end{equation*}

we can take real and imaginary parts to get independent solutions. Thus

\begin{equation} x_h(t) = e^{\alpha t} \bigl[ c_1 \cos(\beta t) + c_2 \sin(\beta t) \bigr], \qquad \text{if } \lambda=\alpha \pm i\beta\text{.}\tag{4.2.2} \end{equation}
