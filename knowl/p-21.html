We're going to get a little weird now and consider the case \(y'=iy\text{,}\) where \(i^2=-1\text{.}\) As we'll eventually see, this is out of much more than idle curiosity in the long run. In any event, we'll assume that the solution \(y(t)=e^{it}\) just takes on complex values at all times. So we could write it in real and imaginary parts as \(e^{it} = u(t) + iv(t)\text{.}\) But then applying the ODE, we must conclude

\begin{equation*} u'(t) + iv'(t) = i(u(t)+iv(t)) = -v(t) + iu(t)\text{,} \end{equation*}

having applied \(i^2=-1\text{.}\) Equating real and imaginary parts, we conclude the pair of real equations \(u'=-v\text{,}\) \(v'=u\text{.}\) Perhaps you recognize these as old friends, sine and cosine. That is,

\begin{equation} e^{it} = \cos(t) + i\sin(t)\text{.}\tag{1.2.1} \end{equation}

This is Euler's identity. It reveals one of the big surprises of applied math: exponential functions, when you go in an imaginary direction, become sin/cos oscillations. This is easily illustrated within MATLAB, which handles complex values routinely. Note the use of 1i to get the imaginary unit.
