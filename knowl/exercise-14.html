Exercise 1. Heating a house.

Here is an (overly) simple model for heating in a house. Let \(b(t)\) be the temperature of the basement, \(m(t)\) be the temperature of the main living area, and \(a(t)\) be the temperature of the attic. Suppose the ground is at a constant 10 degrees C. We use a Newtonian model to describe how the temperature of the basement evolves due to interactions with the earth and the main floor:

\begin{equation*} \frac{db}{dt} = -k_b (b - 10) - k_{mb} (b-m). \end{equation*}

Similarly, the attic interacts with the air, which we will hold at 2 degrees, and the main floor:

\begin{equation*} \frac{da}{dt} = -k_a (a - 2) - k_{ma} (a-m). \end{equation*}

Finally, suppose the main area interacts mostly with the other levels and experiences input from a heater:

\begin{equation*} \frac{dm}{dt} = -k_{mb} (m - b) - k_{ma} (m - a) + h(t). \end{equation*}

Using the vector variable \(\bx = \bigl[b,m,a\bigr]\text{,}\) write the entire system in the form \(\bx' = \bA \bx + \bff(t)\text{.}\) That is, identify what \(\bA\) and \(\bff(t)\) must be based on the above equations.

Now suppose that the heater is shut off and the three temperatures settle into a steady state (i.e., stop changing in time). Write out an algebraic linear system of equations for the temperatures.
