Suppose \(\bx_1,\ldots,\bx_m\) are homogeneous solutions of the ODE, and we use a linear combination of them to satisfy an initial condition \(\bx(t_0)=\bx_0\text{:}\)

\begin{equation*} c_1 \bx_1 + \cdots + c_m \bx_m = \bx_0\text{.} \end{equation*}

One of our go-to facts will be the equivalence of a linear combination of vectors and matrix-vector multiplication. Here that means we define the \(n\times m\) matrix

\begin{equation*} \bX(t) = \bigl[ \bx_1(t) \: \bx_2(t) \: \cdots \: \bx_m(t) \bigr] \end{equation*}

so that

\begin{equation*} \bX(t_0) \begin{bmatrix} c_1 \\ c_2 \\ \vdots \\ c_m \end{bmatrix} = \bx_0\text{.} \end{equation*}

This is a linear algebraic system for the coefficients \(c_j\text{.}\) We can expect a unique solution for any \(\bx_0\) if and only if \(m=n\) and \(\bX(t_0)\) is invertible-or equivalently, when its columns are a basis for \(\Rn{n}\text{.}\)
