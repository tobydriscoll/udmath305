Suppose we complicate the model by adding in the effect of a predator:

\begin{equation*} \frac{1}{x} \, \dd{x}{t} = a\left(1 - \frac{x}{K}\right) - p(x)\text{,} \end{equation*}

where \(p(x)\) predation per capita. A simple model of predation as increasing from zero to a fixed saturation value as \(x\) changes from zero to infinity is

\begin{equation*} \dd{x}{t} = ax\left(1 - \frac{x}{K}\right) - \frac{x^2}{1+x^2}\text{.} \end{equation*}

There is always a fixed point at \(x=0\text{.}\) Any others are determined by the intersections of a linear function with \(p(x)\text{:}\)

\begin{equation*} a \left(1 - \frac{x}{K}\right) = \frac{x}{1+x^2}\text{.} \end{equation*}

There are two major possibilities. For small enough \(K\text{,}\) there is just one fixed point, as shown in Figure 5.3.3. The origin is unstable and the other fixed point is stable. When \(K\) is sufficiently large, there are three fixed points in addition to the origin, as shown in Figure 5.3.4. The origin is still stable, as is the middle of the three positive fixed points. The other two fixed points are stable. The model suggests we might observe either a low-population or high-population steady state in this scenario.
