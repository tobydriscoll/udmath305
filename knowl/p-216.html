We begin with the homogeneous solution

\begin{equation*} x_h(t) = c_1 g(t) = c_1 \exp\left[\int a(t)\, dt\right]\text{,} \end{equation*}

where \(g(t)\) is the growth factor from (2.4.1). The idea of variation of parameters is to posit that the particular solution can be written by replacing the constant with a function,

\begin{equation*} x_p(t) = k(t) g(t)\text{,} \end{equation*}

and then choose \(k(t)\) to make it so:

\begin{equation*} f(t) = x_p' - a(t) x_p = [k'g+kg'] - a k g = k'g + k[g'-ag]\text{,} \end{equation*}

which, because \(g\) is a homogeneous solution, is simply \(f(t)=k'(t)g(t)\text{.}\) Hence we get a proper particular solution provided

\begin{equation*} k(t) = \int \frac{f(t)}{g(t)}\, dt \text{.} \end{equation*}

Observe that we have to integrate once to get \(g\text{,}\) then again to get \(k\) and thus \(x_p\text{.}\)
