Things really heat up when we discover that it's easy to find a simple set of functions that are mutually orthogonal under the functional definition of inner product. If \(j\) and \(k\) are distinct integers, then

\begin{align*} \inprod{e^{ijx}}{e^{ikx}} \amp = \int_{-\pi}^\pi e^{-ijx}\cdot e^{ikx}\, dx\\ \amp = \int_{-\pi}^\pi e^{i(k-j)x} \, dx \\ \amp = \frac{1}{i(k-j)} \left[e^{i(k-j)x}\right]_{-\pi}^\pi \\ \amp = 0\text{,} \end{align*}

since for any integer \(m\) and real \(x\text{,}\) \(e^{im(x+2\pi)}=e^{imx}e^{2\pi i}=e^{imx}\text{.}\) This orthogonality is the best argument yet for the specialness of complex exponentials.
