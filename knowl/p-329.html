A diagonalization is useful in matrix powers:

\begin{align*} \bA^2 \amp = (\bV \bD \bV^{-1})(\bV \bD \bV^{-1}) = \bV \bD (\bV^{-1}\bV) \bD \bV^{-1} = \bV \bD^2 \bV^{-1} \\ \bA^3 \amp = (\bV \bD \bV^{-1})(\bV \bD^2 \bV^{-1}) = \bV \bD (\bV^{-1}\bV) \bD^2 \bV^{-1} = \bV \bD^3 \bV^{-1} \text{,} \end{align*}

etc. When we these matrix powers into the series definition (3.7.1), we can factor out \(\bV\) on the left and \(\bV^{-1}\) on the right:

\begin{align} e^{t\bA} \amp = \bV\left( \bI + t\bD + \frac{1}{2!}t^2 \bD^2 + \cdots \right) \bV^{-1} \tag{3.7.3}\\ \amp = \bV e^{t\bD} \bV^{-1} \text{.}\tag{3.7.4} \end{align}

Finally, because powers of diagonal matrices are diagonal, applying the series definition to the diagonal matrix \(\bD\) gives something very simple:

\begin{equation*} e^{t\bD} = \begin{bmatrix} e^{t\lambda_1} \amp \amp \amp \\ \amp e^{t\lambda_2} \amp \amp \\ \amp \amp \ddots \amp \\ \amp \amp \amp e^{t\lambda_n} \end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}

This gives us a viable path for computing the exponential of a diagonalizable matrix.
