We are interested in the stability of fixed points, that is, the dynamics close to them. We will use linear approximations of the functions \(F\) and \(G\) near a fixed point:

\begin{align*} F(x,y) \amp \approx F(x_p,y_p) + \pp{F}{x} (x-x_p) + \pp{F}{y} (y-y_p)\\ G(x,y) \amp \approx G(x_p,y_p) + \pp{G}{x} (x-x_p) + \pp{G}{y} (y-y_p)\text{,} \end{align*}

where it is understood that the partial derivatives are all evaluated at \((x_p,y_p)\text{.}\) Given that \((x_p,y_p)\) is a fixed point, two terms above are zero. Finally, define \(u_1(t)=x(t)-x_p\text{,}\) \(u_2(t)=y(t)-y_p\) to arrive at

\begin{align*} \dd{u_1}{t} \amp \approx \pp{F}{x} u_1 + \pp{F}{y} u_2 = a_{11} u_1 + a_{12}u_2\\ \dd{u_2}{t} \amp \approx \pp{G}{x} u_1 + \pp{G}{y} u_2 = a_{21} u_1 + a_{22} u_2\text{.} \end{align*}

We are back at the linear, constant-coefficient system \(\bu'=\bA \bu\text{!}\) The matrix \(\bA\) is called the Jacobian matrix at the fixed point. So it's the eigenvalues of the Jacobian matrix that determine the stability of a steady state.
