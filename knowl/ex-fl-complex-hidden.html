Suppose we are given the purely real problem \(y'+y = 2 \sin(t) - 2 \cos(t)\text{.}\) Formula (3) on page 31 gives a full solution to this problem. The tedious details hide the fact that the essence of the matter is quite simple. A plot of a solution tells the story.

f = @(t) 2*sin(t)-2*cos(t);
dydt = @(t,y) -y + f(t);
t = linspace(0,30,500);
[t,y] = ode45(dydt,t,0);

plot(t,[f(t),y]), grid on
xlabel('t'), ylabel('y(t)')
legend('forcing','solution')

The forcing function is, as we now know, a shifted cosine. You can see that after a short initial stage, the solution is too, though with a different phase and amplitude. The polar complex form tells this story neatly, in the form of \(\alpha\) and \(G\) from above. To wit, for an input at frequency \(\omega\text{,}\)

\begin{equation*} G e^{-i\alpha} = \frac{1}{i\omega + 1}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Here we have \(\omega=1\text{,}\) and thus

\begin{equation*} G e^{-i\alpha} = \frac{1}{i + 1} = \frac{1}{i + 1}\cdot \frac{-i+1}{-i + 1} = \frac{1-i}{2}\text{.} \end{equation*}

The cartesian point \((1/2,-1/2)\) is easy to put into polar form, and we find \(G=1/\sqrt{2}\) and \(\alpha=\pi/4\text{.}\) That is, the particular solution has 70% of the amplitude and lags the driving force by one-eighth of a cycle.

As for the details? The particular solution for \(Re^{i\omega t}\) is \(GR e^{i(\omega t - \alpha)}\text{,}\) as derived above. To recover the given forcing term we need only set \(\omega=1,R=2\) and take the imaginary part minus the real part:

\begin{align*} y \amp = \text{Im} \left[ 2 Ge^{i(t - \alpha)} \right] - \text{Re} \left[ 2 Ge^{i(t - \alpha)} \right]\\ \amp = \text{Im} \left[ \sqrt{2} e^{i(t - \pi/4)} \right] - \text{Re} \left[ \sqrt{2} e^{i(t - \pi/4)} \right] \\ \amp = \sqrt{2} \bigl[\sin(t - \pi/4) - \cos(t - \pi/4)\bigr] \text{.} \end{align*}

We could convert that into a single shifted cosine if required. In this case it happens to get a simpler answer, \(y=-2\cos(t)\text{.}\)
