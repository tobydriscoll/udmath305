By multiplying (1.4.1) through by the integrating factor \(M(t)=e^{-at}\text{,}\) we can render it into a form easily solved by calculus:

\begin{align*} e^{-at} y'(t) - (a e^{-at})y(t) \amp = e^{-at}q(t) \\ \dd{}{t}\bigl[ e^{-at} y(t) \bigr] \amp = e^{-at}q(t) \\ e^{-at} y(t) \amp = C + \int e^{-at}q(t)\, dt \\ \amp = C + \int_0^t e^{-as}q(s)\, ds\text{.} \end{align*}

In the last step above we changed from an indefinite integral to a definite one whose value is zero when \(t=0\text{.}\) (We could have chosen any starting time, but this is the most convenient one.) Then it is easy to see that the integration constant satisfies \(C=y(0)\text{,}\) which is a bit less abstract. Altogether,

\begin{equation} y(t) = e^{at}y(0) + \int_0^t e^{a(t-s)}q(s)\, ds\text{.}\tag{1.4.2} \end{equation}
