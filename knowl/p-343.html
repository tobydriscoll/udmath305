Of course, a different vector on the right-hand side such as \(\bb=\twovec{-3}{6}\) does lie on this line, and we can solve the system in this case. In fact, there are infinitely many ways to do it:

\begin{align*} (1+2c) \twovec{1}{-2} - (2+c)\twovec{2}{-4} \amp = \left( \twovec{1}{-2} - 2 \twovec{2}{-4} \right) + c \left( 2\twovec{1}{-2} - \twovec{2}{-4} \right) \\ \amp = \twovec{-3}{6} + c\cdot \textbf{0}\text{.} \end{align*}

In this situation, the corresponding row picture still has two parallel lines, but they conincide, also indicating the infinite set of solutions. For parallel lines, you can have zero or infinitely many solutions; there is nothing in between.
