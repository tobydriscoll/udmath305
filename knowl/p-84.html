The notion of span gives us a shorthand for expressing the nullspace (equivalently, the general homogeneous solution). For reasons we won't get into, every span is referred to as a space. However, it's important to know that there are infinitely many different ways to express the same space. For instance,

\begin{equation*} \spanset\left(\bx_1\right) = \spanset\left(\bx_1,\bx_1,\bx_1\right)\text{.} \end{equation*}

That's kind of an obvious “cheat”. Here is a more subtle one:

\begin{equation*} \spanset\left( \twovec{1}{0}, \twovec{0}{1}, \twovec{1}{1} \right) = \spanset\left( \twovec{1}{0}, \twovec{0}{1} \right)\text{.} \end{equation*}

The reasoning here is that the first two vectors already span all of \(\Rn{2}\text{,}\) so there's nothing more that the third vector can bring to the table. We have a term for these observations.
