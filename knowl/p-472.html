The failure of ExampleÂ 4.3.4 at \(\omega = 1\) was due to the fact that the \(x_p\) we picked is actually a homogeneous solution. There are additional rules to cover this case, but here is an interesting workaround instead. Suppose we seek a particular solution for \(\omega=1\) as the limit of a particular solution as \(\omega\to 1\text{.}\) We won't use the exact \(x_p\) found in the example, but instead subtract a homogeneous solution from it to get

\begin{equation*} \frac{ \cos(\omega t) - \cos(t) } {1-\omega^2}, \end{equation*}

which is also a particular solution. Then L'Hopital applies in the limit to give us

\begin{equation} x_p(t) = \frac{ -t \sin(\omega t) }{ -2 \omega } = \frac{1}{2}t\sin(t). \tag{4.3.1} \end{equation}

Now, to verify that this is a particular solution in the limiting case, we compute

\begin{align*} x_p \amp = \frac{1}{2}t\sin(t) \\ x_p' \amp = \frac{1}{2}[\sin(t)+t\cos(t)] \\ x_p'' \amp = \frac{1}{2}[\cos(t) + \cos(t) - t\sin(t)], \end{align*}

so that \(x_p''+x_p=\cos(t)\) as required.
