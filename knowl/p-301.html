As you can see, converting from the complex to the real form is quite an algebraic slog. It's easier to become comfortable with the complex exponentials themselves. Observe that

\begin{equation} e^{(a+i \omega)t} = e^{at} (\cos \omega t + i \sin \omega t)\text{.}\tag{3.5.1} \end{equation}

Reading from right to left, we have the product of \(e^{i\omega t}\text{,}\) which goes around and around the unit circle at constant angular velocity \(\omega\) , and \(e^{at}\text{,}\) which is an amplitude that exponentially grows or decays (or stays fixed if \(a=0\)).
