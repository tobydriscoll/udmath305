Example 4.4.1.

When a 2 kg mass is hung on a spring, the spring stretches by 0.25 m. What is the natural frequency of the mass-spring system? Suppose the mass is pulled down 0.2 m past equilibrium and then thrown upward at 1 m/s. What is the amplitude of the motion?

Hooke's Law for a spring states that \(F=k x\text{,}\) so we find the spring constant from \(k=F/x=2g/0.25=8g\text{,}\) where \(g=9.8\) m per second squared. The ODE for free motion of the system is thus

\begin{align*} 2 x'' + 8g x \amp = 0 \\ x'' + 4g x \amp = 0, \end{align*}

from which we identify the natural frequency

\begin{equation*} \omega_0 = \sqrt{4g} = 2\sqrt{g} \approx 6.26 \text{s}^{-1}. \end{equation*}

We can apply the initial conditions directly to the amplitude-phase form:

\begin{align*} -0.2 \amp = x(0) = R\cos(0-\theta) = R\cos(\theta) \\ 1 \amp = x'(0) = -\omega_0 R\sin(0-\theta) = \omega_0 R\sin(\theta) \end{align*}

Therefore,

\begin{equation*} R = \sqrt{ [R\cos(\theta)]^2 + [R\sin(\theta)]^2 } = \sqrt{ 0.04 + \omega_0^{-2} } \approx 0.256 \text{m}. \end{equation*}

We could go on to find the phase by taking a ratio to get

\begin{equation*} \tan(\theta) = \frac{1}{-0.2\omega_0}. \end{equation*}

In solving for \(\theta\text{,}\) you have to do a “four-quadrant” arctan. Observe that \((R\cos\theta,R\sin\theta)\) lies in the second quadrant, so \(\theta\) has to be between \(\pi/2\) and \(\pi\text{.}\) One finds that \(\theta\approx 2.47\text{.}\)
