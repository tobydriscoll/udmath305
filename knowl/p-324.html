We don't even have to compute the eigenvalues to figure out the stability. If you add the roots of the quadratic formula, you can confirm that

\begin{equation*} \lambda_1 + \lambda_2 = T\text{.} \end{equation*}

If you multiply them, you find

\begin{equation*} \lambda_1 \lambda_2 = D\text{.} \end{equation*}

Suppose the eigenvalues are real. The only way to keep them both negative is if \(T\lt 0\) and \(D \gt 0\text{;}\) if one is zero and the other negative, then \(T \lt 0\text{,}\) \(D=0\text{.}\) Now suppose the eigenvalues are \(\alpha \pm i\beta\) with nonzero \(\beta\text{.}\) We find that \(D\gt 0\text{,}\) and that \(\alpha \leq 0\) if and only if \(T \leq 0\text{.}\)
