You can easily verify that \(\bD^2=\diag(\lambda_1^2,\lambda_2^2,\dots,\lambda_n^2)\text{,}\) \(\bD^3=\diag(\lambda_1^3,\dots,\lambda_n^3)\text{,}\) etc. So the power series becomes

\begin{equation*} e^{t\bD} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 + t\lambda_1 + \frac{1}{2}(t\lambda_1)^2 + \cdots \amp \amp \amp \\ \amp 1 + t\lambda_2 + \frac{1}{2}(t\lambda_2)^2 + \cdots \amp \amp \\ \amp \amp \ddots \amp \\ \amp \amp \amp 1 + t\lambda_n + \frac{1}{2}(t\lambda_n)^2 + \cdots \end{bmatrix}. \end{equation*}

The diagonal terms become \(e^{t\lambda_i}\) thanks to the power series of \(e^x\text{.}\)
