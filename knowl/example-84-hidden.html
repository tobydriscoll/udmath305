To find the general soluton of \(x'' - 4x + 4x = 5\cos(t)\text{,}\) we note that the characteristic polynomial is \(s^2-4s+4=(s-2)^2\text{.}\) We take transforms to obtain

\begin{equation*} X(s) = \frac{sx(0)+x'(0)}{(s-2)^2} + \frac{5s}{(s^2+1)(s-2)^2} . \end{equation*}

The PFD of this has the real form

\begin{equation*} X(s) = \frac{A}{s-2} + \frac{B}{(s-2)^2} + \frac{Cs+D}{s^2+1}. \end{equation*}

The first two terms constitute the homogeneous solution, and we zero out their coefficients and replace \(X\) with \(X_p\text{.}\) Equating to the earlier expression and clearing denominators gives

\begin{equation*} (sx(0)+x'(0))(s^2+1) + 5s = (Cs+D)(s-2)^2. \end{equation*}

We don't care about the initial values and want to make them vanish. So we choose an imaginary value of \(s\text{:}\)

\begin{align*} s = i \amp \Rightarrow 5i = (Ci+D)(i-2)^2 = (3-4i)(D+Ci) = (3D+4C) + i(3C-4D) \text{.} \end{align*}

Equating the real and imaginary parts gives \(4C+3D=0\text{,}\) \(3C-4D=5\text{.}\) Now our particular solution satisfies

\begin{equation*} X_p(s) = \frac{3}{5} \frac{s}{s^2+1} - \frac{4}{5} \frac{1}{s^2+1}. \end{equation*}

Hence

\begin{equation*} x_p(t) = \frac{3}{5} \cos(t) - \frac{4}{5} \sin(t) \end{equation*}

and finally

\begin{equation*} x(t) = c_1 e^{2t} + c_2 t e^{2t} + \frac{3}{5} \cos(t) - \frac{4}{5} \sin(t). \end{equation*}
