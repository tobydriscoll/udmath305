In order to define the product \(\bA\bB\text{,}\) we require that the number of columns in \(\bA\) is the same as the number of rows in \(\bB\text{.}\) Otherwise, the product is considered undefined. So let \(\bA\) be \(m\times n\) and \(\bB\) be \(n\times p\text{.}\) One way to describe \(\bA\bB\) is as a generalization of the matrix-vector product:

\begin{equation*} \bA\mathbf{B} = \bA \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{b}_1 \amp \mathbf{b}_2 \amp \cdots \amp \mathbf{b}_p \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \bA\mathbf{b}_1 \amp \bA\mathbf{b}_2 \amp \cdots \amp \bA\mathbf{b}_p \end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}

In words, a matrix-matrix product is the horizontal concatenation of matrix-vector products involving the columns of the right-hand matrix. Note that when we multiply \(m\times n\) by \(n\times p\text{,}\) the result is \(m\times p\text{.}\)
