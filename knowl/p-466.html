This is a good moment to observe that there are three equivalent ways to express harmonic motion:

\begin{align*} \text{complex exponential} \amp \qquad c_1 e^{i\omega_0 t} + \overline{c_1} e^{-i\omega_0 t} \\ \text{sin and cos} \amp \qquad a_1 \cos(\omega_0 t) + a_2 \sin(\omega_0 t) \\ \text{amplitude and phase} \amp \qquad R \cos(\omega_0 t - \theta) \end{align*}

We have seen before how Euler's identity connects the first two, but the third one is new. Applying a trig identity to this new form yields

\begin{equation*} R \cos(\theta) \cos(\omega_0 t) + R \sin(\theta) \sin(\omega_0 t), \end{equation*}

which shows that \((R,\theta)\) merely expresses the Cartesian point \((a_1,a_2)\) in polar form. In any of the expressions, there are two real arbitrary constants that are determined by initial conditions, if those are given.
