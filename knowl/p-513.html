We reconsider \(x''-x=24 e^{3t}\) with the goal of finding a general solution. As before, we obtain

\begin{equation*} X(s) = \frac{sx(0) + x'(0)}{(s-1)(s+1)} + \frac{24}{(s-1)(s+1)(s-3)}. \end{equation*}

Without manipulating this further for now, we know that we seek a PFD in the form

\begin{equation*} X(s) = \frac{A}{s-1} + \frac{B}{s+1} + \frac{C}{s-3}. \end{equation*}

Consider that this inverts to

\begin{equation*} x(t) = Ae^t + Be^{-t} + Ce^{3t}. \end{equation*}

The first two terms are due to the eigenvalues and are part of the homogeneous solution. Their coefficients are arbitrary in the general solution. It's the coefficient \(C\) that is of interest to us; i.e., a particuar solution. Suppose we chose the initial conditions in a way that zeroed out the homogeneous terms. Then

\begin{equation*} \frac{sx(0) + x'(0)}{(s-1)(s+1)} + \frac{24}{(s-1)(s+1)(s-3)} = \frac{C}{s-3}. \end{equation*}

Clearing the denominators leads to

\begin{equation*} (sx(0)+x'(0))(s-3) + 24 = C(s-1)(s+1). \end{equation*}

We don't actually care about the numerical values of the initial conditions. When we set \(s=3\text{,}\) they disappear and we get \(24=8C\text{.}\) Right away, then, we know that the general solution is

\begin{equation*} x(t) = x_h(t) + x_p(t) = c_1 e^{t} + c_2 e^{-t} + 3 e^{3t}. \end{equation*}
