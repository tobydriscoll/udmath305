Section 2.1 Introduction

¶

A differential equation is an equation that has some derivatives in it. (Duh.) The variable whose derivative is taken is called the dependent variable and is considered the "unknown" of the differential equation. We'll be concerned with the case where there is only one independent variable (a "with respect to" variable in the denominator of a derivative), so there are no partial derivatives and we say the equation is an ordinary differential equation, or ODE. For discussions and theory we will stick to \(x\) and \(t\) as the dependent and independent variables, but in general these are determined by what is being modeled by the ODE.

If only first derivatives appear (that is, no \(x''\text{,}\) \(x'''\text{,}\) etc.), it's a first-order ODE. We're going to stick to this kind of ODE for a while.

The easiest ODE in the world is

\begin{equation*} \dd{x}{t} = 0\text{.} \end{equation*}

A solution of this ODE is a function \(x(t)\) that satisfies the ODE. Perhaps you can work out that the solution here is \(x(t)=C\) for an arbitrary constant \(C\text{.}\) As dumb as this example is, it points out something important right away.

Fact 2.1.1.

Solutions to ODEs are not unique.

More general, but not really any more new to you, is the ODE \(\dd{x}{t}=f(t)\text{.}\) Both sides can be integrated to give \(x(t) = \int f(t)\,dt + C\text{.}\)

Things get interesting only when both \(x\) and \(x'\) appear in the equation. We'll use four examples or archetypes to begin the discussion.

Example 2.1.2. Constant growth rate.
\begin{equation*} \dd{x}{t} = x \end{equation*}

It's trivial to check that \(x=e^t\) is one solution of \(x'=x\text{,}\) i.e., it makes the ODE a true equation. As a mathematical model, the ODE states that the rate of change in \(x\) is equal to \(x\) itself, and the result is exponential growth: the solution grows larger, which increases the rate of growth, and so on in a feedback loop.

Exponential growth is most naturally plotted on a log-linear graph, in which case the solution is a straight line with positive slope. Because of the log scale, a “difference” in the \(y\)-coordinate is actually a multiplicative factor in the solution. Thus each unit of time applies a constant multiplier of growth.

Example 2.1.4. Constant decay rate.
\begin{equation*} \dd{x}{t} = -x \end{equation*}

Now the model states that the rate of change is negative when \(x\) is positive (and vice versa). So a positive starting value will decrease, but the rate of decrease will then lessen, etc. From this qualitative description alone it's impossible to tell whether the solution ever reaches zero--at which point its rate of change would be zero.

But we can end the suspense by easily verifying that \(x=e^{-t}\) is a solution. This function asymptotically approaches zero as \(t\to\infty\text{,}\) which we call exponential decay. In other, related problems we will find that solutions can decay (or "relax") to any value, not just zero.

Exponential decay is most naturally plotted on a log-linear graph, in which case the solution is a straight line with negative slope.

Example 2.1.6. Variable growth rate.
\begin{equation*} \dd{x}{t} = 2tx \end{equation*}

In this case we are back to a positive growth rate for positive solution values. In fact, the growth rate increases with time as well as with the solution itself. You can verify that \(x=e^{t^2}\) is a solution, and indeed this grows at a "super-exponential" rate.

Example 2.1.8. Nonlinear growth.
\begin{equation*} \dd{x}{t} = x^2 \end{equation*}

This is our first example featuring a nonlinear term in the variable \(x\text{.}\) As a rule, nonlinear ODEs are much harder to solve and have less mathematical structure than linear problems. This particular example isn't so tough, though; you can check that \(x=1/(1-t)\) is a solution. Note that the growth is even faster--literally off the charts as \(t\to 1\text{.}\)

You might wonder about the validity of any mathematical model that leads to an infinite result in finite time. But this particular ODE describes, for instance, the evolution of the slope of the line of sight to an airplane flying straight over you. When the airplane is directly overhead, the slope is infinite. So while the model becomes mathematically invalid at that moment, it does describe a real physical situation.
