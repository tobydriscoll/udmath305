Section 5.1 Main idea

In vector calc you learned about the dot product between two vectors. This generalizes into \(n\) dimensions. Suppose \(\bu\) and \(\bv\) are real column vectors of length \(n\text{.}\) Then their dot product, or what we will call the inner product, is

\begin{equation*} \langle \bu, \bv \rangle = \bu \cdot \bv = \overline{u_1} v_1 + \overline{u_2} v_2 + \cdots \overline{u_n} v_n = \sum_k \overline{u_k} v_k\text{.} \end{equation*}

Notice that we introduced complex conjugates onto the \(u_k\) terms. One motivation is that it makes the inner product of a vector with itself guaranteed to be a nonnegative real number:

\begin{equation*} \langle \bu, \bu \rangle = \sum_k \overline{u_k} u_k = \sum_k |u_k|^2 \ge 0\text{.} \end{equation*}

In fact we define a function called a norm by \(\| \bu \| = \sqrt{ \langle \bu, \bu \rangle}\text{.}\) This generalizes familiar Euclidean distance into \(n\) dimensions and complex-valued vectors.

Subsection 5.1.1 Orthogonal vectors

One of the most useful facts about the inner product in three dimensions is the geometric formula

\begin{equation*} \inprod{\bu}{\bv} = \| \bu \| \, \| \bv \| \, \cos(\theta)\text{,} \end{equation*}

where \(\theta\) is the angle between the vectors. We can take this to mean the definition of angle in higher/complex dimensions. This is most important to us when the angle is a right angle, i.e., \(\inprod{\bu}{\bv} = 0\text{,}\) in which case we say the vectors are orthogonal (generalizing "perpendicular").

Orthogonal vectors are convenient for many reasons. One of those is that it's easy to express any vector as a linear combination of them. Suppose the vectors \(\bv_1,\ldots,\bv_n\) are pairwise orthogonal in \(n\)-dimensional space, and \(\bx\) is any other vector in \(\mathbb{C}^n\text{.}\) If it's possible to write

\begin{equation*} \mathbf{x} = c_1 \bv_1 + c_2 \bv_2 + \cdots c_n \bv_n\text{,} \end{equation*}

then we can find relatively simple expressions for these constants, because

\begin{equation*} \inprod{\bv_k}{\bx} = c_1 \inprod{\bv_1}{\bx} + \cdots + c_n \inprod{\bv_n}{\bx} = c_k \inprod{\bv_k}{\bv_k}\text{,} \end{equation*}

where the last step follows from the orthgonality. Hence

\begin{equation*} c_k = \frac{\inprod{\bv_k}{\mathbf{x}}}{\|\bv_k\|^2}, \quad k=1,\ldots,n\text{.} \end{equation*}

This formula expresses the geometric fact that the inner product can be used to find the projections of a vector onto orthogonal directions.

Example 5.1.1

You can verify quickly that \(\twovec{2}{-1}\) and \(\twovec{1}{2}\) are orthogonal in 2-D. Then we can verify, for instance,

\begin{equation*} \twovec{3}{5} = \frac{6-5}{2^2+(-1)^2} \twovec{2}{-1} + \frac{3+10}{2^2+1^2} \twovec{1}{2} = \frac{1}{5} \twovec{2+13}{-1+26}\text{.} \end{equation*}

(Not addressed above is the question of whether every \(\bx\) can be expressed that way. By our standard interpretation of linear combinations, the answer must be yes if the matrix \(\bigl[ \bv_1 \, \cdots \, \bv_n \bigr]\) is nonsingular. It's actually not hard to produce the inverse explicitly in the orthogonal case, but I won't go into the details here. In a fuller linear algebra course there would be other ways to establish this fact as well.)

We can ease the formulas a little if we define

\begin{equation*} \bu_k = \frac{\bv_k}{\|\bv_k\|}, \quad k=1,\dots,n\text{,} \end{equation*}

that is, we normalize the original orthogonal set of vectors. Then

\begin{equation*} \bx = \inprod{\bu_1}{\bx} \bu_1 + \cdots + \inprod{\bu_n}{\bx} \bu_n\text{.} \end{equation*}

So far this might not look like a compelling idea. But we're about to do the exact same representation with functions.

Subsection 5.1.2 Orthogonal functions

It's remarkable that we can generalize the inner product from finite vectors to functions. As usual, summation turns into integration. Let's restrict attention for now to functions on the domain \([-\pi,\pi]\text{.}\) Then we define the inner product of two functions \(u(x),v(x)\) as

\begin{equation*} \inprod{u}{v} = \int_{-\pi}^\pi \overline{u(x)} v(x)\, dx\text{.} \end{equation*}

This definition turns immediately into strange-sounding but mathematically meaningful notions of the "length" (norm) of a function and the angle between two functions.

Things really heat up when we discover that it's easy to find a simple set of functions that are mutually orthogonal under the functional definition of inner product. If \(j\) and \(k\) are distinct integers, then

\begin{align*} \inprod{e^{ijx}}{e^{ikx}} \amp = \int_{-\pi}^\pi e^{-ijx}\cdot e^{ikx}\, dx\\ \amp = \int_{-\pi}^\pi e^{i(k-j)x} \, dx \\ \amp = \frac{1}{i(k-j)} \left[e^{i(k-j)x}\right]_{-\pi}^\pi \\ \amp = 0\text{,} \end{align*}

since for any integer \(m\) and real \(x\text{,}\) \(e^{im(x+2\pi)}=e^{imx}e^{2\pi i}=e^{imx}\text{.}\) This orthogonality is the best argument yet for the specialness of complex exponentials.

Subsection 5.1.3 Fourier series

In order to normalize the orthogonal complex exponential functions, we need to calculate the inner product in the case of \(j=k\text{:}\)

\begin{align*} \inprod{e^{ijx}}{e^{ijx}} \amp = \int_{-\pi}^\pi e^{-ijx}\cdot e^{ijx}\, dx\\ \amp = 2\pi \end{align*}

So we define

\begin{equation*} u_k(x) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{ikx}, \quad k=0,\pm 1,\pm 2,\dots\text{.} \end{equation*}

(Does it bother you that this set has infinitely many members? Sorry, but functions are naturally infinite-dimensional.) Then this suggests the formulas

\begin{equation*} f(x) = \sum_{k=-\infty}^\infty c_k u_k(x),\quad c_k = \int_{-\pi}^\pi \overline{u_k}(x) f(x)\, dx\text{.} \end{equation*}

This is our first encounter with what is called a Fourier series for \(f\text{.}\) We do have to be cautious when we approach infinite sums. While this formula isn't straightforwardly true for every function you could think of, it's true enough to be one of the most monumental developments in the history of the analysis of functions.
