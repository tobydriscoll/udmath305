Section 4.3 Multiplying matrices and vectors

¶

From section 4.3 (skip "Elimination matrices"), with a little from 4.1 also.

It's time to go all-in on matrices.

An \(m\times n\) matrix \(\bA\) is a rectangular \(m\)-by-\(n\) array of numbers called elements or entries. The numbers \(m\) and \(n\) are called the row dimension and the column dimension, respectively; collectively they describe the size of \(\bA\text{.}\) We say \(\bA\) belongs to \(\mathbb{R}^{m\times n}\) if its entries are real or \(\mathbb{C}^{m\times n}\) if they are complex-valued.

A square matrix has equal row and column dimensions. A row vector has dimension \(1\times n\text{,}\) while a column vector has dimension \(m \times 1\text{.}\) By default, a vector is understood to be a column vector, and we use \(\mathbb{R}^n\) or \(\mathbb{C}^n\) to denote spaces of vectors. An ordinary number may be called a scalar.

We will mainly be dealing with scalars, vectors and square matrices. Please keep in mind that "scalar/vector" and "constant/function" are separate attributes. All four combinations of them are possible. I use capital letters in bold to refer to matrices, and lowercase bold letters for vectors. The bold symbol \(\boldsymbol{0}\) may refer to a vector of all zeros or to a zero matrix, depending on context.

To refer to a specific element of a matrix, I use the uppercase name of the matrix without boldface, as in \(A_{24}\) to mean the \((2,4)\) element of \(\bA\text{.}\) (This is not universal practice, but it is most like MATLAB.) To refer to an element of a vector, I use just one subscript, as in \(x_3\text{.}\) If you see a boldface character with one or more subscripts, then you know that it is a matrix or vector that belongs to a numbered collection. We sometimes find it useful to refer to the individual columns of a matrix as vectors. My convention is to use a lowercase bold version of the matrix name, with a subscript to represent the column number. Thus, \(\bA_1,\bA_2,\ldots,\bA_n\) are the columns of the \(m\times n\) matrix \(\bA\text{.}\)

Adding and subtracting matrices and vectors is dead simple: if two things are the same size, you add/subtract them elementwise; otherwise, the operation is not permitted. (Caution: MATLAB does let you do that sometimes, and it's a great source of confusing, subtle bugs.)

However, multiplication and "division" are far more complicated for matrices than they are for scalars.

Subsection 4.3.1 Vectors: Dot products

Suppose \(\bu\) and \(\bv\) are real column vectors of length \(n\text{.}\) Then their dot product (or inner product, or scalar product is

\begin{equation*} \bu \cdot \bv = u_1 v_1 + u_2 v_2 + \cdots u_n v_n = \sum_i u_i v_i\text{.} \end{equation*}

This is equivalent to what you were used to doing in two and three dimensions in vector calculus. In fact, a well-known formula there holds here too:

\begin{equation*} \bu \cdot \bv = \| \bu \| \, \| \bv \| \, \cos(\theta)\text{,} \end{equation*}

where \(\|\) is used to indicate Euclidean length (or norm) and \(\theta\) is the angle between the vectors. This is most important to us when the angle is a right angle, i.e., \(\bu\cdot\bv = 0\text{,}\) in which case we say the vectors are orthogonal (meaning perpendicular).

Subsection 4.3.2 Matrix times vector

Suppose \(\bA\) is \(m\times n\) and \(\bx\) is \(n\times 1\text{,}\) a column vector. The column dimension of \(\bA\) has to be the same as the row dimension in \(\bb\text{.}\) We can define the product \(\bA\bx\) two equivalent ways. Each interpretation has its uses.

First we think about \(\bA\) acting on \(\bx\). The product \(\by=\bA\bx\) is an \(m\times 1\) vector whose \(i\)th element is the dot product of \(\bx\) with the \(i\)th row of \(\bA\text{.}\) More symbolically,

\begin{equation*} y_i = \sum_{k=1}^n A_{ik} x_k, \qquad i=1,\ldots,m\text{.} \end{equation*}

We can also interpret the product as \(\bx\) acting on \(\bA\text{.}\) Specifically,

\begin{align*} \bA \mathbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix} \displaystyle \sum_k A_{1k}x_k \\[2mm] \displaystyle \sum_k A_{2k}x_k \\ \vdots\\ \displaystyle \sum_k A_{mk}x_k \end{bmatrix} \amp = x_1 \begin{bmatrix} A_{11}\\A_{21}\\\vdots\\A_{m1} \end{bmatrix} + x_2 \begin{bmatrix} A_{12}\\A_{22}\\\vdots\\A_{m2} \end{bmatrix} + \cdots + x_n \begin{bmatrix} A_{1n}\\A_{2n}\\\vdots\\A_{mn} \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp = x_1 \bA_1 + x_2 \bA_2 + \cdots + x_n \bA_n\text{.} \end{align*}

In words, we say that \(\bA\bx\) is a linear combination of the columns of \(\bA\text{,}\) with the combination coefficients coming from \(\bx\text{.}\)

This is a critical tie-in to linear systems. We previously observed that a linear system of equations could be interpreted as being about linear combinations of the columns of the coefficient matrix.

Fact 4.3.1

The linear system with matrix \(\bA\) and vector \(\bb\) is about solving \(\bA\bx=\bb\) for \(\bx\text{.}\)

Subsection 4.3.3 Matrix times matrix

The last and most general case for us to consider is the product \(\bA\bB\text{.}\) As in the matrix-vector case, there is a constraint on the sizes: the number of columns of \(\bA\) has to be the same as the number of rows in \(\bB\text{.}\) So if \(\bA\) is \(m\times n\) and \(\bB\) is \(n\times p\text{,}\) we can define the product \(\bC = \bA\bB\) as the \(m\times p\) matrix with entries defined by

\begin{equation*} C_{ij} = \sum_{k=1}^n A_{ij}B_{jk}, \qquad i=1,\ldots,m, \quad j=1,\ldots,p\text{.} \end{equation*}
Example 4.3.2

Let

\begin{equation*} \bA = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp -1 \\ 0 \amp 2 \\ -3 \amp 1 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad \mathbf{B} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \amp -1 \amp 0 \amp 4 \\ 1 \amp 1 \amp 3 \amp 2 \end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Then

\begin{align*} \bA\mathbf{B} \amp= \begin{bmatrix} (1)(2) + (-1)(1) \amp (1)(-1) + (-1)(1) \amp (1)(0) + (-1)(3) \amp (1)(4) + (-1)(2) \\ (0)(2) + (2)(1) \amp (0)(-1) + (2)(1) \amp (0)(0) + (2)(3) \amp (0)(4) + (2)(2) \\ (-3)(2) + (1)(1) \amp (-3)(-1) + (1)(1) \amp (-3)(0) + (1)(3) \amp (-3)(4) + (1)(2) \end{bmatrix} \\ \amp = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp -2 \amp -3 \amp 2 \\ 2 \amp 2 \amp 6 \amp 4 \\ -5 \amp 4 \amp 3 \amp -10 \end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{align*}

One way to think about the definition of matrix multiplication is as a generalization of the matrix-vector product. It turns out that

\begin{equation*} \bA\mathbf{B} = \bA \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{b}_1 \amp \mathbf{b}_2 \amp \cdots \amp \mathbf{b}_n \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \bA\mathbf{b}_1 \amp \bA\mathbf{b}_2 \amp \cdots \amp \bA\mathbf{b}_n \end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}

In words, a matrix-matrix product is the horizontal concatenation of matrix-vector products involving the columns of the right-hand matrix.

Example 4.3.3

Returning to the previous example, note that

\begin{equation*} \bA \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} = 2 \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ -3 \end{bmatrix} + 1 \begin{bmatrix} -1 \\ 2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ -5 \end{bmatrix}\text{,} \end{equation*}

and so on.

Subsection 4.3.4 Properties

First, the bad news. In the previous subsection we computed the product \(\bA\bB\text{,}\) where the matrices are \(3\times 2\) and \(2\times 4\text{,}\) to get a result that is \(3\times 4\text{.}\) However, the product \(\bB\bA\) isn't even defined, because \(4\neq 3\text{.}\) Moreover, even when both \(\bA\bB\) and \(\bB\bA\) are defined, we cannot expect them to be equal.

Fact 4.3.4

Matrix multiplication is not commutative. That is, the order of terms in a product affects the result.

In a product of matrices (and vectors), position matters. You cannot change the ordering (without justification)! You may have to unlearn some old habits.

Fortunately, other familiar properties of scalar multiplication do come along for the ride:

  1. \((\bA\bB)\bC=\bA(\bB\bC)\)
  2. \(\bA(\bB+\bC) = \bA\bB + \bA\bC\)
  3. \((\bA+\bB)\bC = \bA\bC + \bB\bC\)
  4. \(\alpha(\bB+\bC) = \alpha\bB + \alpha\bC\)

The associative law is especially valuable; many algorithms and results in computational linear algebra rely on it.
