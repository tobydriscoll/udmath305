Section 4.7 Repeated and complex poles

¶

Subsection 4.7.1 Repeated poles and resonance

Repeated poles in the Laplace transform of a solution appear in the form of denominator terms such as \((s-a)^k\) or \((s^2+\omega^2)^k\) with \(k \gt 1\text{.}\) They can arise in two ways: from repeated eigenvalues in the homogeneous or unforced ODE, or because the forcing function has a pole in common with one of the eigenvalues (e.g., perfect resonance).

Consider inverting the double real pole

\begin{equation*} X(s) = \frac{1}{(s-a)^2}\text{.} \end{equation*}

The key is to note that \(X(s)=-F'(s)\text{,}\) where \(F(s) = (s-a)^{-1}\) is the transform of \(e^{at}\text{.}\) From the transform definition we compute

\begin{align*} -F'(s) \amp = -\frac{d}{ds} \int_0^\infty f(t) e^{-s t}\, dt\\ \amp = \int_0^\infty t f(t) e^{-s t}\, dt\\ \amp = \lx[ t f(t) ] = \lx[ t e^{at} ] \text{.} \end{align*}

If we keep differentiating in \(s\) we find

\begin{equation} \lx[t^n e^{at} ] = \frac{n!}{(s-a)^{n+1}}.\label{so-eq-repeatedexp}\tag{4.7.1} \end{equation}

The special case \(a=0\) allows us to transform any power of \(t\text{,}\) and therefore any polynomial.

Example 4.7.1.

The critically damped oscillator \(x'' + 6x' + 9=0\) has a double eigenvalue at \(-3\text{.}\) To find the solution with \(x(0)=0\text{,}\) \(x'(0)=6\text{,}\) we use the transform to find

\begin{equation*} [s^2X(s) - 6] + 6[sX(s)] + 9[X(s)] = 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad X(s) = \frac{6}{(s+3)^2}. \end{equation*}

Hence (4.7.1) applies with \(n=1\text{,}\) and

\begin{equation*} x(t) = 6t e^{-3t}\text{.} \end{equation*}

If you look carefully at the derivation of (4.7.1), you will see that the identity of \(f(t)\) played no role in the key steps. As shown in Table 4.6.1, we can repeat the calculation for a generic \(F(s)\text{:}\)

\begin{equation*} \lx[t^n f(t) ] = (-1)^n F^{(n)}(s), \qquad n=1,2,3,\dots. \end{equation*}

Take a moment to appreciate this. Earlier we found that a derivative in \(t\) essentially corresponds to multiplying by \(s\text{.}\) Up to a change in sign, a derivative in \(s\) corresponds to multiplying by \(t\text{!}\)

Example 4.7.2.

The undamped oscillator \(x''+9x=\sin(3t)\) has a perfect resonance. The transform suggests the particular solution

\begin{equation*} X_p(s) = \frac{3}{(s^2+9)^2}\text{.} \end{equation*}

We are close to using the fact that

\begin{equation*} \frac{d}{ds} \left[ \frac{3}{s^2+9} \right] = \frac{-6s}{(s^2+9)^2}. \end{equation*}

In fact, if we designate \(G(s)=2sX_p(s)\text{,}\) then we know that

\begin{equation*} \lx[ t \sin(3t) ] = -\frac{d}{ds} \left[ \frac{3}{s^2+9} \right] = G(s), \end{equation*}

and therefore \(g(t)=t\sin(3t)\text{.}\) Next, note that

\begin{equation*} 2\lx[x_p'(t)] = 2[sX_p(s)-X_p(0)] = G(s). \end{equation*}

Hence \(x_p'(t) = \frac{1}{2} t \sin(3t)\text{,}\) and integration by parts yields

\begin{equation*} x_p(t) = \frac{1}{18} [ \sin(3t) - 3t\cos(3t) ]\text{.} \end{equation*}

As the preceding example shows, dealing with repeated poles is no easy feat, especially when quadratic factors are in the denominator. Another tactic is to break up those factors as imaginary poles and use standard partial fractions.

Example 4.7.3.

Again we consider how to invert

\begin{equation*} X_p(s) = \frac{3}{(s^2+9)^2} = \frac{3}{(s-3i)^2(s+3i)^2} \text{.} \end{equation*}

The PFD has four terms that work out as

\begin{equation*} X_p(s) = \frac{1}{36} \left[ \frac{i}{s+3i} - \frac{i}{s-3i} - \frac{3}{(s+3i)^2} - \frac{3}{(s-3i)^2} \right]. \end{equation*}

These easily invert using complex exponentials:

\begin{equation*} x_p(t) = \frac{1}{36} \left[ i\bigl(e^{-3it}-e^{3it}\bigr) - 3 t \bigl(e^{-3it}+e^{3it}\bigr) \right]. \end{equation*}

Now Euler' formula leads back to the same result,

\begin{equation*} x_p(t) = \frac{1}{18} [ \sin(3t) - 3t\cos(3t) ]\text{.} \end{equation*}

Subsection 4.7.2 Complex poles

Complex poles in the transform correspond to complex exponentials in the time domain. When they occur in conjugate pairs, the entire expression can be cast in real terms. It can help to use the shift formula

\begin{equation*} \lx[e^{at}f(t)] = F(s-a)\text{.} \end{equation*}

We should have enough information from the ODE to know what to choose as the real shift \(a\text{.}\)

Example 4.7.4.

Suppose \(x''+2x'+10x=6\delta(t-1)\text{.}\) A particular solution with zero initial conditions has

\begin{equation*} X_p(s) = \frac{6e^{-s}}{s^2+2s+10}\text{.} \end{equation*}

The quadratic formula tells us the homogeneous eigenvalues \(-1\pm 3i\text{,}\) so we assume a shift of \(-1\) and designate \(X_p(s)=G(s+1)\text{.}\) Thus

\begin{equation*} G(s) = X_p(s-1) = 6e \frac{e^{-s}}{s^2-2s+1+2s-2+10} = 6e \frac{e^{-s}}{s^2+9}. \end{equation*}

Now we can apply the Shift Theorem to \(G\) and get

\begin{equation*} g(t) = 6e H(t-1) v(t-1), \end{equation*}

where \(v(t)=\sin(3t)/3\text{.}\) When we unwind the definitions, we get

\begin{equation*} x_p(t) = e^{-t} g(t) = 2 e^{-t+1} H(t-1)\sin(3t-3). \end{equation*}

Are we having fun yet?

Now consider \(x''+2x'+10x=40\text{.}\) We get

\begin{equation*} X_p(s) = \frac{40}{s(s^2+2s+10)} \text{.} \end{equation*}

We have the same eigenvalues and so define \(X_p(s)=G(s+1)\) to get

\begin{equation*} G(s) = X_p(s-1) = \frac{40}{(s-1)(s^2+9)}. \end{equation*}

This is amenable to the PFD

\begin{equation*} G(s) = \frac{A}{s-1} + \frac{B+Cs}{s^2+9}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Clearing denominators, we get

\begin{equation*} 40 = A(s^2+9) + (B+Cs) (s-1)\text{.} \end{equation*}

If we set \(s=1\) we immediately see that \(A=4\text{.}\) That leaves

\begin{equation*} 40 = (4+C)s^2 +(B-C)s + 36-B\text{.} \end{equation*}

Now we can read off \(B=-4\text{,}\) \(C=-4\text{.}\) Thus

\begin{equation*} g(t) = 4e^{t} - 4 \cos(3t) - \frac{4}{3}\sin(3t). \end{equation*}

Finally,

\begin{equation*} x_p(t) = e^{-t}g(t) = 4 - 4 e^{-t}\cos(3t) - \frac{4}{3} e^{-t}\sin(3t). \end{equation*}

For a harmonically forced case such as \(x''+2x'+10x=\cos(2t)\text{,}\) we have

\begin{equation*} X_p(s) = \frac{s}{(s^2+4)(s^2+2s+10)}\text{.} \end{equation*}

The best first step is to decompose

\begin{equation*} X_p(s) = \frac{A+Bs}{s^2+4} + \frac{C+Es}{s^2+2s+10}\text{.} \end{equation*}

The first term leads directly to \(\sin(2t)\) and \(\cos(2t)\text{,}\) while the second would once again be shifted to get \(s^2+9\) in the denominator. The details are left to the proverbial reader.

The drawback to Laplace transforms as a one-stop solver is what we observed earlier: the particular solution we find virtually always includes some terms from the homogeneous solution. Mathematically, the poles of \(X_p(s)\) include those of the transfer function and the forcing function. This combination makes the partial fraction decompositions lengthier, to say the least. Undetermined coefficients is a less sexy but simpler way to solve concrete problems in most cases, especially if one seeks a general solution without initial conditions to consider at the end.
