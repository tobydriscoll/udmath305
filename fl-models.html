Section 2.6 Modeling with first-order ODEs

¶

First-order ODEs are often used to model situations of growth and decay. The linear problem \(x'=ax+f(t)\) is a prototype for many important problems:

Population

Population \(x(t)\) of organisms has a constant net per-capita birth (or death) rate

Interest

Money amount \(x(t)\) grows at fixed positive interest rate \(a\)

Radioactivity

\(x(t)\) is the mass of a radioactive isotope, and \(a \lt 0\)

Pharmacokinetics

\(x(t)\) is the amount of a drug being metabolized in the body

Newtonian cooling

If \(x(t)\) is the temperature of a body kept in an environment at fixed temperature \(E\text{,}\) then the difference \(z(t)=x(t)-E\) satisfies \(z'=a z\) for a cooling rate \(a \lt 0\)

To belabor the obvious, positive \(a\) represents growth and negative \(a\) leads to decay. In the simplest contexts, the rate \(a\) is constant, but in most models a variable rate is more realistic.

Interest rates in the real world may be quoted yearly, or quarterly, or according to any other finite time period. The rate in an ODE model is the continuously compounded rate.

The units of the derivative \(dx/dt\) are those of \(x\) divided by those of \(t\text{.}\) Let's write these as \(X/T\text{.}\) Additive terms all need to have the same units, so both \(f(t)\) and \(a(t)x\) have those units as well. Consequently \(a\) has units \(1/T\) and has various interpretations when inverted:

  • When \(a \lt 0\text{,}\) the time \(\tau=-1/a\) is the relaxation time or characteristic time. If there is no forcing, then \(x(\tau)= e^{-1} x(0) \approx 0.37 x(0)\text{.}\)
  • In radioactivity it's more common to use \(t_h=-\ln(2)/a\text{,}\) which is the half-life. That's because \(\exp(at_h)=1/2\text{,}\) so half of the radioactive isotope is depleted in that much time.
  • Similarly, in population or another growth situation with \(a \gt 0\text{,}\) the time \(t_D=\ln(2)/a\) is the doubling time. Note that populations and interest don't grow arithmetically, like \(1,2,3,4,\ldots\text{,}\) but geometrically, like \(1,2,4,8,\ldots\text{.}\)
Example 2.6.1. First-order pharmacokinetics.

According to R. Newton et al., “Plasma and salivary pharmacokinetics of caffeine in man,” European Journal of Clinical Pharmacology 21 (1981), pp. 45–52, caffeine in the bloodstream approximately satisfies first-order kinetics, though the half-life varies a great deal from one person to the next.

Suppose \(t_h=6\) hours. We can calculate \(a=-\ln(2)/t_h\approx -0.116\) per hour, and then the predicted effects of one cup of coffee are \(x(t) = e^{-0.116t}x(0)\text{.}\) You can check that an equivalent, more direct expression is

\begin{equation*} x(t) = 2^{-t/t_h} x(0)\text{.} \end{equation*}

A continuously stirred tank reactor (CSTR) appears often in chemical engineering. One ideally assumes that the contents of the tank are mixed perfectly and instantaneously at all times. Then one writes an ODE that expresses mass balance.

Example 2.6.2. Stirred tank.

A 200 L tank contains 10 kg of dye. Pure water is added at a rate of 4 L per minute, while the mixture is drained at the same rate. How much dye is in the tank after 10 minutes?

Solution

Let \(x(t)\) be the mass of dye in the tank. The trick is to realize how rapidly it is being removed, and that depends on the time-varying concentration, \(x(t)/200\text{.}\) Specifically,

\begin{equation*} \dd{x}{t} = - \frac{4 \text{ L}}{\text{minute}} \cdot \frac{x\, \text{ kg}}{200\, \text{ L}} = -0.02 x \text{ kg/min}\text{.} \end{equation*}

So \(x(t)=e^{-0.02 t}x(0)\) and \(x(10)=10 e^{-0.2}\) kg.

(These problems can also be solved by using concentration, not mass, as the dependent variable. Either is fine, so long as you are consistent.)

Exercises 2.6.1 Practice with models

¶
1. Tank with changing volume.

A 300 gal capacity tank initially holds 100 gal of a brine solution at 1 lb/gal. A stream of brine at 2 lb/gal is poured into the tank at 3 gal/min, and an exit stream of the well-mixed solution leaves the tank at 2 gal/min. Find the amount of salt in the tank at the time it is about to overflow.

Solution

To write a rate equation for the amount \(x(t)\) of salt in the tank at time \(t\text{,}\) we use the principle of conservation of mass. We assume no reaction takes place in the tank, so that up to the moment of overflow we have

\begin{equation*} \text{rate of change of } x = \text{(rate of salt in)} - \text{(rate of salt out)} \end{equation*}

Let \(V(t)=\) the volume of the mixture at time \(t\text{.}\) Then

\begin{align*} \text{rate of salt in} \amp = 2 \text{ lb/gal }\times 3 \text{ gal/min }= 6 \text{ lb/min}\\ \text{rate of salt out} \amp = \frac{x}{V} \text{ lb/gal }\times 2 \text{ gal/min }= \frac{2}{V}\,x \text{ lb/min.} \end{align*}

Hence,

\begin{equation*} \frac{dx}{dt} = 6 - \frac{2}{V}\,x. \end{equation*}

But the volume of the mixture changes as well (rate of change = rate in - rate out):

\begin{equation*} \frac{dV}{dt}= 3 - 2 = 1 \text{ gal/min } \; \Rightarrow\; V(t) = (t+100) \text{ gal, } \end{equation*}

where we have used the initial volume (100 gal) for the constant of integration. Note that the time for overflow is when \(V=300\) gal, i.e. 200 min.

The initial amount of salt in the tank is 100 lb. Using the volume expression, we obtain an IVP for \(x(t)\text{,}\) namely

\begin{align*} \frac{dx}{dt} \amp = 6 - \frac{2}{t+100} x,\quad 0\lt t \le 200,\\ x(0) \amp = 100. \end{align*}

The ODE in standard form is

\begin{equation*} x' + \frac{2}{t+100}x = 6, \end{equation*}

which can be solved by variation of parameters:

\begin{align*} g(t) \amp =\exp\left(\int -\frac{2}{t+100}\,dt\right)=(t+100)^{-2}, \\ k(t) \amp = \int 6(t+100)^2\, dt = 2(t+100)^3, \\ x(t) \amp = c_1(t+100)^{-2} + 2(t+100)^{3-2}. \end{align*}

Using the initial condition, we obtain \(c_1=-100^3\text{.}\) The time of overflow is \(t=200\) min, so finally we get

\begin{equation*} x(200)=2(200+100)-\frac{100^3}{(200+100)^2}=\frac{5300}{9}\approx 589 \text{ lb.} \end{equation*}
2. Polluted lake.

A well-circulated lake contains 1000 kL of water that is initially at a concentration of 2 kg/kL of a pollutant. Water from the effluent of a factory enters the lake at the rate of 5 kL/h with a concentration of 7 kg/kL of the pollutant. Polluted water flows out of the lake through an outlet at the rate of 2 kL/h. Determine the amount of pollutant in the lake as a function of time. (Ignore the fact that the lake may eventually overflow, as we are interested in a relatively short time.)

Hint

Write the IVP satisfied by the mass of pollutant using the principle of mass conservation. Note that the volume of the lake is not constant, and be very careful to match units.

Solution

It's clear that given the net fluid inflow rate of 3 kL/h, the lake volume is given by \(V(t)=1000 + 3t\) kL at time \(t\) hours. Let \(x(t)\) be the mass of pollutant in the lake at \(t\) hours in kg/kL. Mass conservation implies

\begin{equation*} \frac{dx}{dt} \text{ kg/h} = (5)(7) \text{ kg/h} - 2 \frac{x}{V} \text{ kg/h}, \end{equation*}

or

\begin{equation*} x' + \frac{2x}{3t+1000} = 35. \end{equation*}

This equation readily solved through variation of parameters:

\begin{align*} g(t) \amp =\exp\left(\int -\frac{2}{3t+1000}\,dt\right)=(3t+1000)^{-2/3}, \\ k(t) \amp = \int 35(3t+1000)^{2/3} \, dt = 7(3t+1000)^{5/3}, \\ x(t) \amp = c_1(3t+1000)^{-2/3} + 7(3t+1000). \end{align*}

The initial condition is that \(x(0)/V(0)=2\text{,}\) so

\begin{equation*} 2 = \frac{c_1\cdot(1000)^{-2/3} + 7000}{1000}\text{,} \end{equation*}

which implies \(c_1=-5(1000)^{5/3}=-5\times 10^5\text{.}\) Thus

\begin{equation*} x(t)=7(3t+1000) - \frac{5\times 10^5}{(3t+1000)^{2/3}} \; \text{kg/kL.} \end{equation*}
3. Cooling coffee.

A mug of coffee at 90 C is put in a room kept at 20 C. After 10 minutes the coffee has cooled by \(7\) C. When will the coffee reach 60 C?

Solution

In terms of the notation above, we have

\begin{equation*} \frac{dx}{dt} = a (x-20), \quad x(0)=90\text{.} \end{equation*}

We could also pose it for the temperature difference \(z=x-20\) as \(z'=az\text{.}\) Either way it's a linear problem with solution

\begin{equation*} x(t) = Ce^{at} + 20\text{.} \end{equation*}

We have two unknown parameters in the solution, but also two unused pieces of information. We determine \(C\) from the initial value:

\begin{equation*} x(0)= 90 \Rightarrow C=70\text{.} \end{equation*}

To get a value for \(a\text{,}\) we use

\begin{equation*} 83 = x(7) = 70e^{10a}+20\text{,} \end{equation*}

or \(a=0.1\ln(0.9) \approx -0.01054\) per min. Hence the coffee will reach 60 C when \(40 = 70e^{at}\text{,}\) or \(t\approx 53.1\) minutes.

4. Two mugs of coffee.

A more sophisticated model for the cooling of coffee in a well-insulated mug is

\begin{equation*} z' = -rSz, \end{equation*}

where \(z(t)\) is the difference between the coffee temperature and the room temperature, \(S\) is the exposed surface area of the coffee, and \(r\) is a positive rate constant. Thus, a wider mug that exposes more surface area will cause the coffee to cool faster.

Suppose Fred and Wilma each pour out a mug of coffee that is at 85 degrees C. Wilma's mug has a diameter that is \(\sqrt{2}\) times as large as Fred's. Both mugs are left sitting out for 30 minutes in a room that is at 25\({}^\circ\) C, at which point Fred's coffee is at 60 degrees. What is the temperature of Wilma's coffee at the same moment?

Solution

Let's write the IVPs for the two mugs as

\begin{align*} z_F' \amp = -r S_F z_F, \quad z_F(0) = 85-25, \\ z_W' \amp = -r S_W z_W, \quad z_W(0) = 85-25, \end{align*}

in which we also know that \(S_W=2S_F\) (because area is proportional to the square of the radius). The solutions are

\begin{align*} z_F(t) \amp = 60 \exp(-r S_F t), \\ z_W(t) \amp = 60 \exp(-r 2 S_F t). \end{align*}

We are informed that \(z_F(30) = 60-25=35\text{,}\) and we need to find \(z_W(30)\text{.}\) But notice that

\begin{equation*} 60 z_W(t) = 60\cdot 60 \cdot \exp(-r S_F t) \cdot \exp(-r S_F t) = z_F(t)^2. \end{equation*}

Hence \(z_W(30)=z_F(30)^2/60=35^2/60\approx 20.4\text{,}\) and Wilma's coffee is at \(45.4^\circ\) C.

Note that we don't have enough information to figure out \(S_F\) and \(r\) separately. We could have solved for their product, which would give us enough information to finish the problem. However, when you find yourself taking a log to get a rate constant and then exponentiating the rate times a different time, it's often possible to skip over the step of finding the rate constant.

5. Pharmacokinetics.

Pharmaceutical companies need to track the concentration of certain drugs throughout the body in order to determine dosage, toxicity, etc. This is usually done via a compartment model: the body is divided into compartments, and a balance law is formulated in each compartment to account for the transport, consumption and elimination of the drug.

Consider a simple pill of antihistamine taken by a person. The pill goes to the GI tract where it dissolves and the antihistamine diffuses through to the bloodstream (BS), and it is then eliminated by chemical reactions or through the kidneys. Let \(x(t)\) and \(y(t)\) be the concentrations of the drug in the GI tract and the bloodstream, respectively. Assume the rate of clearance from each compartment is proportional to the drug concentration in that compartment. Formulate an IVP for the two concentrations. Assuming the initial concentrations are \(x(0)=A\) and \(y(0)=0\text{,}\) solve the IVPs.

Solution

Using the given assumptions and mass balances we obtain the system

\begin{align*} \frac{dx}{dt} \amp =-k_1 x, \quad x(0)=A,\\ \frac{dy}{dt} \amp = k_1 x - k_2y, \quad y(0)=0. \end{align*}

Although this takes the form of a system of two equations for two dependent variables, we can solve the first one for \(x\) independently and then put that result into the second ODE. Specifically, \(x(t)=A\exp(-k_1 t)\text{,}\) so

\begin{equation*} y' + k_2 y = k_1 A e^{-k_1t}. \end{equation*}

Let us use undetermined coefficients here. The homogeneous solution is \(y_h(t)=c_1e^{-k_2 t}\text{,}\) and the particular solution has to take the form \(y_p(t)=B e^{-k_1 t}\text{.}\) We find the coefficient through placement into the ODE:

\begin{equation*} (-k_1B e^{-k_1t}) + k_2 (B e^{-k_1 t}) = k_1 A e^{-k_1t}, \end{equation*}

which implies \(B=k_1A/(k_2-k_1)\text{,}\) provided the rate constants are different. (Whatever solution method is chosen would have to deal with that case separately.) Thus

\begin{equation*} y(t) = c_1e^{-k_2 t} + \frac{k_1 A}{k_2-k_1} e^{-k_1 t}. \end{equation*}

We find the unknown \(c_1\) through the condition

\begin{equation*} 0 = y(0) = c_1 + \frac{k_1 A}{k_2-k_1}, \end{equation*}

and finally

\begin{equation*} y(t) = \frac{k_1 A}{k_2-k_1} \left( e^{-k_1 t} - e^{-k_2 t} \right). \end{equation*}
