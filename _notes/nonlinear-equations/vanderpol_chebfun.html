Note that the "spring" term here is linear, and the nonlinearity is in what we usually think of as the "damping" term. However, when this term is actually feeding energy in, only to switch back to damping when . As a result, the motion is attracted to a periodic solution called a limit cycle.
mu = 0.1;
vdP = chebop(@(y) diff(y,2)+y-mu*(1-y^2)*diff(y),[0,30],[2.5 0],[]);
y =vdP\0; plot(y)
xlabel t, ylabel y
title('vdP, \mu = 0.1')
arrowplot(y,diff(y))
xlabel y, ylabel('y''')
title('vdP, \mu = 0.1')
axis equal
As μ increases, the convergence to the limit cycle is more rapid.
mu = 1;
vdP = chebop(@(y) diff(y,2)+y-mu*(1-y^2)*diff(y),[0,15],[2.5 0],[]);
y =vdP\0;
arrowplot(y,diff(y))
xlabel y, ylabel('y''')
title('vdP, \mu = 1')
axis equal
In addition, the period lengthens, and the system undergoes alternating slow/fast motions.
mu = 8;
vdP = chebop(@(y) diff(y,2)+y-mu*(1-y^2)*diff(y),[0,80],[2.5 0],[]);
y =vdP\0;
plot(y)
xlabel t, ylabel y
title('vdP, \mu = 8')
