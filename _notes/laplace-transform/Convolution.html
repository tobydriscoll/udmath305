Convolutions

Here's a very oscillatory function.
f = randnfun(.2,[0 50]);
plot(f,[0 20])
title('Original "signal" f')
We let w be a 2-second window function.
w = chebfun({1,0},[0 2 10]);
plot(w),xlim([0 5])
title('Window function w')
Convolving f with w performs a moving 2-second averaging of f.
g =conv(w,f);
plot(g,[0 20])
title('Once averaged')
As you can see, a lot of the high-frequency oscillation was smoothed out by the averaging, leaving mostly lower-frequency trends. Another convolution will smooth it even more.
h = conv(w,g);
plot(h,[0 20])
title('Twice averaged')

Blurring images

A common application of convolution is in image processing. Think of an image as a (discrete) 2D function of pixel intensity.
s = load('clown.mat');
F = rgb2gray(ind2rgb(s.X, s.map));
imagesc(F), colormap(gray(256))
title('Original image')
Lens blur can be well modeled as convolution with a 2D Gaussian such as the following.
[I,J] = ndgrid(-8:8);
G = exp(-(I.^2+J.^2)/8);
imagesc(G)
title('Gaussian bump')
Here's the result of a 2D convolution.
H = conv2(F,G);
imagesc(H)
title('Blurred by convolution')
Modern "deep learning" tools for image recognition depend heavily on what are called convolutional neural network layers, each of which essentially performs 2D convolution with a weighted bump whose weights are determined by training with known images.
